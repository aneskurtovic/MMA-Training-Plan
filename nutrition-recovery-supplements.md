# MMA Fighter Nutrition, Recovery & Supplementation Guide
## Evidence-Based Protocols for an 87-90kg Combat Athlete

## 1. NUTRITION

### 1.1 Daily Caloric Targets (87-90kg / ~88kg reference weight)

| Goal | kcal/kg/day | Daily Total |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Maintenance (moderate training) | 35-40 | 3,080-3,520 |
| High-volume training days | 40-45 | 3,520-3,960 |
| Slight deficit (slow recomp) | 30-35 | 2,640-3,080 |
| Rest day deficit | 25-30 | 2,200-2,640 |

**Source:** ISSN Position Stand on Nutrition for Combat Sports (Reale et al., 2025) recommends 25-70 kcal/kg/day depending on training phase, with 25-30 kcal/kg as a baseline and higher for intensive training blocks.

### 1.2 Macronutrient Targets (88kg reference)

| Macro | g/kg | Training Day | Rest Day |
|-------|------|-------------|----------|
| Protein | 2.0-2.2 | 176-194g | 176-194g |
| Carbs | 4-5 (training) / 3 (rest) | 352-440g | 264g |
| Fat | 0.8-1.0 | 70-88g | 70-88g |

**Calculated daily totals:**

**Training Day (~3,300 kcal target for slow recomp):**
- Protein: 185g (740 kcal) - kept at 2.1g/kg
- Carbs: 400g (1,600 kcal) - ~4.5g/kg
- Fat: 85g (765 kcal) - ~1.0g/kg
- Total: ~3,105 kcal

**High-Volume Double Session Day (~3,600 kcal):**
- Protein: 185g (740 kcal)
- Carbs: 440g (1,760 kcal) - 5g/kg
- Fat: 88g (792 kcal)
- Total: ~3,292 kcal (add intra-workout carbs to reach ~3,600)

**Rest Day (~2,600 kcal for slight deficit):**
- Protein: 185g (740 kcal) - protein stays high
- Carbs: 264g (1,056 kcal) - 3g/kg
- Fat: 85g (765 kcal)
- Total: ~2,561 kcal

**Key principle:** Protein stays constant. Carbs and fats cycle based on training demand. This is supported by the ISSN recommendation that macronutrients should not drop below: carbs 3.0g/kg, protein 1.2-2.0g/kg (higher during deficit: 1.8-2.7g/kg), fat 0.5-1.0g/kg.

**Sources:**
- Reale R, et al. "International society of sports nutrition position stand: nutrition and weight cut strategies for mixed martial arts and other combat sports." JISSN, 2025. DOI: 10.1080/15502783.2025.2467909
- Kasper AM, et al. "Nutrition, supplementation and weight reduction in combat sports: a review." PMC, 2021. PMC8334642

### 1.3 Meal Timing Protocols

**Pre-Training Meal (2-3 hours before):**
- 1-2g/kg carbs (88-176g), 0.3-0.4g/kg protein (26-35g), low fat (<15g)
- Focus on easily digestible carbs to avoid GI distress during grappling
- Examples:
  - 150g white rice + 150g chicken breast + small banana
  - 100g oats (dry weight) + 1 scoop whey + honey
  - 2 slices sourdough toast + 3 eggs + fruit

**Pre-Training Snack (30-60 min before, if needed):**
- 30-50g fast-digesting carbs, minimal fat/fiber
- Banana + rice cake, or sports drink

**Intra-Workout Nutrition (double session days / sessions >90 min):**
- 30-60g carbs per hour (sports drink, diluted juice, or carb powder)
- 500-700mg sodium per liter of fluid
- Sip 600-1200ml/hour depending on sweat rate
- Example: 40g maltodextrin + pinch of salt in 750ml water per hour

**Post-Training Window (within 1-2 hours):**
- 0.3-0.5g/kg protein (26-44g) + 1-1.5g/kg carbs (88-132g)
- This is a priority window, but total daily intake matters more than precise timing
- Examples:
  - Whey shake (40g) + 2 bananas + oats
  - 200g chicken + 250g white rice + vegetables
  - Greek yogurt (300g) + granola + berries

### 1.5 Hydration Protocol

**Baseline:**
- Minimum 35-45ml/kg/day = 3.1-4.0L for an 88kg athlete
- Spread throughout the day, not chugged in large volumes

**Training additions:**
- Pre-training: 500ml water in the 2 hours before
- During training: 600-1200ml/hour depending on sweat rate and conditions
- Post-training: Replace 150% of fluid lost (weigh before/after to estimate sweat loss)
- Include electrolytes when training >60 min or in heat

**Monitoring:** Urine color should be pale straw. Dark urine = dehydrated. Completely clear = possibly overhydrated.

**Source:** ACSM Position Stand on Exercise and Fluid Replacement (Sawka et al., 2007). DOI: 10.1249/mss.0b013e31802ca597

## 2. SUPPLEMENTS (Evidence-Based Only)

### 2.1 Creatine Monohydrate - STRONG EVIDENCE

**Dose:** 5g/day, every day (loading phase optional: 20g/day for 5-7 days, split into 4x5g)

**Evidence Summary:**
- The most extensively studied and effective ergogenic supplement available (ISSN Position Stand, Kreider et al., 2017)
- Increases phosphocreatine stores by 20-40%, directly fueling the ATP-PC system used in 6-36 second high-intensity bursts (exactly the work pattern in MMA rounds)
- Meta-analysis shows mean strength improvement of ~8% and power output improvement of ~14% vs placebo
- A 2025 systematic review specific to combat sports (Ashtary-Larky et al., 2025) confirmed increases in peak power, especially when combined with resistance training over 6+ weeks
- No evidence of kidney damage, dehydration, or cramping in healthy individuals at recommended doses
- May cause 1-2kg water weight gain initially (intracellular, not bloating)

**Combat sports consideration:** The 1-2kg weight gain is relevant for weight class athletes. Best used during training camps well before weight cuts. The performance benefits outweigh the minor weight increase for most fighters.

**Timing:** Any time of day. Can be taken with a meal for slightly better absorption. Consistency matters more than timing.

**Sources:**
- Kreider RB, et al. "International Society of Sports Nutrition position stand: safety and efficacy of creatine supplementation in exercise, sport, and medicine." JISSN, 2017. PMC5469049
- Ashtary-Larky D, et al. "Creatine Supplementation in Combat Sport Athletes: A Narrative Systematic Review." PubMed, 2025. PMID: 40755023
- Li Y, et al. "Advantages of different dietary supplements for elite combat sports athletes: a systematic review and Bayesian network meta-analysis." Scientific Reports, 2024. DOI: 10.1038/s41598-024-84359-3

### 2.2 Caffeine - STRONG EVIDENCE

**Dose:** 3-6mg/kg body mass = 264-528mg for an 88kg fighter
- Start at the lower end (3mg/kg = ~260mg, roughly a strong double espresso)
- Some athletes respond to as low as 2mg/kg (~175mg)

**Timing:** 45-60 minutes pre-training (peak plasma levels at ~60 min)

**Effects:**
- Improved reaction time, alertness, power output, and endurance
- Reduced perceived exertion
- Combat-sport-specific: improved technical performance in taekwondo athletes at 3mg/kg (Lopes-Silva et al.)
- Mixed results in judo at higher doses, suggesting sport-specificity

**Caveats for combat athletes:**
- Half-life is 3-7 hours. Avoid if training after 4 PM and sleep is a priority
- Habitual users develop tolerance; consider 7-day washout before competition
- Do NOT exceed 6mg/kg - diminishing returns and increased side effects (anxiety, HR elevation, GI distress)
- Individual genetic variation (CYP1A2 gene) affects metabolism speed

**Source:** Guest NS, et al. "International society of sports nutrition position stand: caffeine and exercise performance." JISSN, 2021. PMC7777221. DOI: 10.1186/s12970-020-00383-4

### 2.3 Electrolytes - SITUATIONAL EVIDENCE

**When needed:**
- Training sessions >60 minutes
- Double session days
- Hot/humid conditions
- Heavy sweaters (>1L/hour sweat rate)

**Dosing (per liter of fluid consumed during training):**
- Sodium: 500-700mg (up to 1000mg for heavy sweaters)
- Potassium: 78-195mg
- Magnesium: 50-100mg

**Practical approach:**
- Add 1/4 tsp salt (~575mg sodium) per 750ml water during training
- Or use a commercial electrolyte mix (check sodium content - most are too low)
- Post-training: sodium-containing foods (salted rice, broth, etc.)

**Source:** ACSM Position Stand on Exercise and Fluid Replacement (Sawka et al., 2007). PMID: 17277604

### 2.4 Vitamin D - MODERATE EVIDENCE

**Dose:** 3,000-5,000 IU/day (75-125mcg)
- Get blood levels tested first (25-hydroxyvitamin D). Target: 40-60 ng/mL (100-150 nmol/L)
- Most athletes are deficient, especially at higher latitudes or those training indoors

**Effects:**
- Bone health and fracture prevention
- Muscle function and force production
- Immune function (reduced upper respiratory infections)
- May reduce inflammation

**Take with:** A fat-containing meal for better absorption (fat-soluble vitamin)

**Sources:**
- Chiang CM, et al. "Effects of Vitamin D Supplementation in Elite Athletes: A Systematic Review." PMC10768611, 2023
- Heffernan SM, et al. "The Importance of Vitamin D and Magnesium in Athletes." Nutrients, 2025. DOI: 10.3390/nu17101655

### 2.5 Magnesium - MODERATE EVIDENCE

**Best forms:**
- Magnesium glycinate (best absorbed, calming, good for sleep - take at night)
- Magnesium citrate (well absorbed, may have mild laxative effect)
- AVOID magnesium oxide (poor bioavailability, ~4% absorption)

**Dose:** 400-500mg elemental magnesium per day
- Athletes have higher requirements than the general RDA of 300-400mg
- Most athletes are deficient through diet alone
- Split dose: 200mg morning, 200-300mg evening (before bed)

**Effects:**
- ATP production and energy metabolism
- Muscle contraction and relaxation (may reduce cramps)
- Sleep quality improvement
- Electrolyte balance
- Bone health

**Source:** Heffernan SM, et al. "The Importance of Vitamin D and Magnesium in Athletes." Nutrients, 2025. PMC12114196

### 2.6 Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA) - MODERATE EVIDENCE

**Dose:** 2-3g combined EPA+DHA per day
- Aim for higher EPA ratio for anti-inflammatory effects
- Example: 1.5g EPA + 1g DHA daily

**Effects:**
- Reduced post-exercise inflammation and muscle soreness (DOMS)
- May accelerate recovery between sessions
- Cardiovascular health
- Potential neuroprotective effects (relevant for combat sports with head trauma)
- Joint health

**Take with:** Meals containing fat for absorption

**Source:** IOC Consensus Statement on Dietary Supplements and the High-Performance Athlete (Maughan et al., 2018). PMC5867441

## 3. RECOVERY

### 3.1 Sleep - THE #1 Recovery Tool

**Target:** 8-9 hours per night (some evidence supports up to 10 hours for elite athletes during heavy training blocks)

**Evidence:** Sleep deprivation impairs muscular strength, power output, endurance, reaction time, and cognitive function. Stanford basketball study showed extended sleep (10 hours) improved sprint times and shooting accuracy by 9%+ (Mah et al., 2011).

**Sleep Hygiene Protocol:**
1. **Consistent schedule:** Same bed/wake time +/- 30 min, even weekends
2. **Cool room:** 18-20C (65-68F) - core temp drop triggers sleep onset
3. **Dark room:** Blackout curtains, no LEDs. Melatonin suppressed by light >50 lux
4. **No screens 60 min before bed** (or use blue-light blockers as minimum)
5. **No caffeine after 2 PM** (or 8+ hours before intended sleep)
6. **No large meals within 2 hours of bed** (small casein/cottage cheese snack is fine)
7. **Magnesium glycinate 200-300mg** 30-60 min before bed
8. **Post-training evening routine:** warm shower, foam rolling, stretching - signals wind-down
9. **Limit alcohol** - even small amounts fragment sleep architecture and suppress REM/deep sleep
10. **Napping:** 20-30 min naps before 3 PM can supplement night sleep (don't nap longer or later)

**Sources:**
- Vitale KC, et al. "Sleep Hygiene for Optimizing Recovery in Athletes: Review and Recommendations." IJSPP, 2019. PMC6988893
- Mah CD, et al. "The effects of sleep extension on the athletic performance of collegiate basketball players." Sleep, 2011
- Bonnar D, et al. "Sleep and Athletic Performance: Impacts on Physical Performance, Mental Performance, Injury Risk and Recovery, and Mental Health." Sports Med, 2023. PMC9960533

### 3.2 Foam Rolling / Self-Myofascial Release

**Protocol:**
- **When:** Immediately post-training (within 20 min) and every 24 hours during recovery
- **Duration:** 1-2 minutes per muscle group, 60-90 seconds per tender spot
- **Pressure:** Moderate - should be uncomfortable but not painful (6-7/10)
- **Speed:** Slow, controlled rolls. Pause on trigger points

**Priority Muscles for MMA Athletes:**
| Muscle Group | Duration | Why (MMA-specific) |
|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| Thoracic spine | 2 min | Posture from guard/clinch work |
| Lats | 1-2 min each | Pulling/clinch/guard retention |
| Hip flexors / TFL | 1-2 min each | Kicking, guard work, sprawls |
| Quads / IT band | 2 min each leg | Stance work, sprawls, takedowns |
| Glutes / piriformis | 1-2 min each (lacrosse ball) | Hip escapes, kicking, stance changes |
| Calves / peroneals | 1 min each | Footwork, stance |
| Forearm extensors | 1 min each | Grip fatigue from grappling |
| Pecs / anterior delts | 1 min each | Framing, pushing, posture correction |

**Total session:** 15-20 minutes

**Evidence:** Foam rolling post-exercise reduces DOMS at 24, 48, and 72 hours. Does NOT impair subsequent performance when done post-training. Pre-training rolling may transiently reduce force output - keep it brief (<30 sec/muscle) if used in warm-up.

### 3.3 Contrast Showers / Contrast Water Therapy

**Protocol:**
1. Start with 2 minutes warm/hot water (38-40C / 100-104F)
2. Switch to 1 minute cold water (10-15C / 50-59F)
3. Repeat for 3-5 cycles
4. Always END on cold

**Total duration:** 12-20 minutes

**Evidence:** The evidence is mixed. Cold water immersion (CWI) alone has stronger support than contrast therapy for reducing muscle damage markers and perceived soreness. However, contrast therapy may aid perceived recovery and reduce peripheral edema through the "vascular pumping" mechanism (alternating vasoconstriction/vasodilation).

**Practical recommendation:** Use contrast showers as a convenient recovery tool. They are unlikely to harm recovery and may provide subjective benefit. Full cold water immersion (10-15C for 10-15 min) has stronger evidence but requires a tub/barrel.

**Important caveat:** Avoid cold exposure immediately after strength/hypertrophy sessions if the goal is muscle growth. Cold blunts the inflammatory signaling needed for adaptation. Save cold therapy for after skill sessions or on rest days. After pure S&C sessions, warm shower only.

**Source:** Higgins TR, et al. "Evidence-based post exercise recovery in combat sports: a narrative review." BMC Sports Sci Med Rehabil, 2020. PMID: 33092328

### 3.4 When to Use Ice vs Heat

| Situation | Use | Protocol |
|-----------|-----|----------|
| Acute injury (0-72 hours), swelling, sharp pain | ICE | 15-20 min on, 40+ min off, repeat. Wrap in cloth. |
| Chronic muscle tightness, stiffness | HEAT | 15-20 min, moist heat preferred. Before stretching. |
| Post-training general soreness (no injury) | Neither or CONTRAST | Contrast showers. Or just active recovery. |
| Joint inflammation (after hard grappling) | ICE | 15 min, 2-3x/day for 48 hours |
| Pre-training warm-up of stiff areas | HEAT | 10 min heat pack on area, then dynamic warm-up |
| After strength training (want adaptation) | NEITHER | Avoid ice - let inflammation do its job |
| Bruises/contusions from sparring | ICE (first 48h) then HEAT | Ice to limit swelling, then heat to promote blood flow |

### 3.5 Active Recovery Day Structure

**Purpose:** Promote blood flow, reduce stiffness, maintain mobility without adding training stress. Keep heart rate below 120-130 BPM (Zone 1-2).

**Sample Active Recovery Day:**

| Time | Activity | Duration |
|------|----------|----------|
| Morning | 20-30 min walk or light cycling | 30 min |
| Morning | Foam rolling full body | 15-20 min |
| Morning | Mobility/yoga flow (hip openers, thoracic rotation, shoulder CARs) | 20-30 min |
| Afternoon | Light swimming or hot tub/sauna | 20-30 min |
| Evening | Contrast shower | 15 min |
| Evening | Static stretching (hamstrings, hip flexors, shoulders) | 10-15 min |
| Night | Magnesium, sleep hygiene routine | - |

**What NOT to do on recovery days:**
- No sparring, no hard rolling, no heavy lifting
- No "just a light session" that turns into full training
- Avoid sitting all day - gentle movement is better than complete rest

### 3.6 Signs of Overtraining / Overreaching

**Early Warning Signs (Functional Overreaching - reversible in 1-2 weeks):**
- Elevated resting heart rate (>5 BPM above baseline on waking)
- Poor sleep quality despite feeling tired
- Decreased grip strength (test with hand dynamometer or just notice grip failing earlier in rolling)
- Mood changes: irritability, apathy, loss of motivation to train
- Persistent muscle soreness lasting >72 hours after a session
- Decreased performance despite consistent training

**Serious Signs (Non-Functional Overreaching / Overtraining Syndrome):**
- Getting sick frequently (>2 colds in 6 weeks) - immune suppression
- Persistent fatigue not relieved by 1-2 rest days
- Hormonal disruption: decreased libido, disrupted menstrual cycle
- Resting heart rate elevated for >1 week
- Loss of appetite
- Weight loss (unintended)
- Mood disorders: depression, anxiety
- Increased injury rate

**Monitoring Protocol:**
1. **Daily:** Record resting HR on waking (before getting out of bed). Use a rolling 7-day average.
2. **Daily:** Rate sleep quality 1-10 and energy 1-10 in a training log
3. **Weekly:** Grip strength test (same time, same hand, 3 attempts)
4. **Weekly:** Subjective wellness questionnaire (mood, motivation, soreness, sleep, stress)
5. **Listen to your body:** 2+ warning signs simultaneously = reduce training load by 40-50% for 5-7 days

**Source:** Meeusen R, et al. "Prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of the overtraining syndrome." European Journal of Sport Science, 2013. Also: Fry AC, et al. "Overtraining in athletes." Sports Medicine, 1998. PMID: 9739537

### 4.2 Concurrent Training & Interference Effect

3. **Wilson JM, Marin PJ, Rhea MR, et al.** "Concurrent training: a meta-analysis examining interference of aerobic and resistance exercises." *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research*, 2012. PMID: [22002517](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22002517/)
   - Key finding: Strength training effect size 1.76, concurrent training 1.44. Running causes more interference than cycling. Recommend 3-6 hour gap between modalities.

4. **Fyfe JJ, Bishop DJ, Stepto NK.** "Interference between concurrent resistance and endurance exercise: molecular bases and the role of individual training variables." *Sports Medicine*, 2014. PMID: [24728927](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24728927/)

### 4.3 Energy Systems in MMA

6. **Franchini E, Cormack S, Takito MY.** "Energy System Contributions during Olympic Combat Sports: A Narrative Review." *Nutrients/Sports*, 2023. [PMC9961508](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9961508/)
   - MMA energy system breakdown: aerobic dominant overall (~70-80% over a fight), but critical moments (takedowns, scrambles, striking exchanges) rely on ATP-PC and glycolytic systems. High lactate values (10-20 mmol/L) measured in MMA competition.

7. **Tota L, Drwal T, Maciejczyk M, et al.** "The Effects of Conditioning Training on Body Build, Aerobic and Anaerobic Performance in Elite Mixed Martial Arts Athletes." *JCMR*, 2019. [PMC6942465](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6942465/)
   - 14-week periodized training: 51% aerobic, 22.4% mixed, 16.2% anaerobic lactic, 10.4% anaerobic alactic.

8. **Kostikiadis IN, et al.** "The Effect of Short-Term Sport-Specific Strength and Conditioning Training on Physical Fitness of Well-Trained Mixed Martial Arts Athletes." *Sports*, 2018. [PMC6090403](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6090403/)
   - Short-term S&C blocks (4-6 weeks) can significantly improve power and strength measures in trained MMA athletes.

### 4.4 Periodization for MMA

9. **Schoenfeld BJ.** "Periodization for Mixed Martial Arts." (Available via [Academia.edu](https://www.academia.edu/34931861/Periodization_for_Mixed_Martial_Arts))
   - Proposes concurrent block periodization model for MMA: accumulation (hypertrophy/aerobic base), transmutation (strength/anaerobic), realization (power/sport-specific).

### 4.5 Strength-to-Weight Ratio Optimization

10. **James LP, et al.** "Strength and Conditioning Considerations for Mixed Martial Arts." (Available via [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/232106158_Strength_and_Conditioning_Considerations_for_Mixed_Martial_Arts))
    - Emphasizes relative strength (strength-to-weight ratio) over absolute strength. Recommends compound lifts (squat, deadlift, pull-ups) tested relative to body weight. Target benchmarks for combat athletes: 1.5-2x BW squat, 1.5-2x BW deadlift, 1-1.5x BW bench.

### 4.6 Recovery Science

11. **Dupuy O, et al.** "Evidence-based post exercise recovery in combat sports: a narrative review." *BMC Sports Sci Med Rehabil*, 2020. PMID: [33092328](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33092328/)
    - Reviews cold water immersion, contrast therapy, compression, massage, sleep, and nutrition for combat sport recovery.

12. **Souza GM, et al.** "Postexercise Physical Recovery Methods for Combat Sports: A Scoping Review." 2025. PMID: [40378882](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40378882/)
    - Most recent scoping review of recovery modalities specific to combat sports.

13. **Carvalho A, et al.** "Biochemical and psychological markers of fatigue and recovery in mixed martial arts athletes during strength and conditioning training." *Scientific Reports*, 2025. DOI: [10.1038/s41598-025-09719-z](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-09719-z)
    - Psychological markers (perceived fatigue, reduced vigor) precede biochemical markers as early warning signs of overtraining.
