# MMA Strength & Conditioning Research Notes
## Key Coaches, Methodologies, and Best Practices

---

## 1. Joel Jamieson (8 Weeks Out) -- Energy Systems Development

### Key Training Principles
- **"Conditioning is sport-specific energy systems development, not just getting tired."** The foundation of his system is understanding and training the three energy systems (aerobic, glycolytic, alactic) according to the specific demands of the fighter's sport and individual physiology.
- The aerobic system is the BASE of all conditioning. If your sport lasts longer than 60 seconds of activity, it has a huge aerobic component -- this includes all MMA rounds.
- Every fighter has different energy system strengths/weaknesses. Programs must be individualized based on testing, not cookie-cutter.
- Recovery IS training. The aerobic system drives recovery between rounds, between explosive bursts, and between training sessions.

### Specific Protocols

**Cardiac Output Method (Aerobic Base)**
- Heart rate: 120-150 BPM (younger athletes 130-150, older 120-150)
- Duration: 30-90 minutes
- Modalities: mobility circuits, light sled drags, farmer's carries, jump rope, cycling, bodyweight circuits, light medicine ball work, battle ropes
- Purpose: increases stroke volume (heart pumps more blood per beat), builds capillary density, improves recovery capacity
- This is Zone 1 -- the "Activation Zone"

**Tempo Intervals**
- Work intervals at ~70% max HR for 10-15 seconds, rest 45-60 seconds
- Repeat for 15-25 minutes
- Builds aerobic power without excessive fatigue

**Threshold Training (Zone 2)**
- HR approximately 150s to mid-160s
- RPE 7-8, cannot hold conversation
- Develops anaerobic threshold -- the point where lactate accumulates faster than it clears

**VO2 Max Training (Zone 3)**
- Highest intensity zone
- Entire cardiovascular system working at maximum oxygen delivery
- Used sparingly, closer to competition

**8-Week Fight Camp Structure**
- Weeks 1-3: Aerobic base emphasis (cardiac output, tempo intervals)
- Weeks 3-5: Threshold development, introduce alactic power
- Weeks 5-7: High-intensity intervals, sport-specific conditioning
- Week 8: Taper -- reduce volume, maintain intensity

### MMA Application
- Alactic system training is key for devastating striking and ground-and-pound power
- Aerobic base enables recovery between explosive exchanges
- Fighters who gas out typically have poor aerobic bases, NOT poor "cardio" in the traditional sense
- Individual assessment determines whether a fighter needs more aerobic base work or more power development

### Notable Concepts
- "Power in MMA depends not just on how strong or fast you are, but how well conditioned your specific energy systems are to produce power when it counts."
- The concept of "energy system fingerprinting" -- every fighter has a unique profile
- Morpheus (his HRV monitoring system) for daily readiness tracking

## 2. Phil Daru (American Top Team S&C Coach)

### Key Training Principles
- **Movement assessment comes FIRST.** Strength cannot be achieved without properly assessing movement capabilities. Dysfunction must be addressed before adding external load.
- Fight camp S&C is about complementing skill training, not competing with it. As fight day approaches, S&C volume must decrease while skill intensity increases.
- Fighters are often weight-training novices -- don't over-complicate exercise selection. Keep movements in the program long enough (minimum 3 weeks) for adaptation.
- Joint integrity is non-negotiable. The body must be armored against the rigors of combat.

### Specific Protocols

**Combat Block Periodization (8-week fight camp)**

**Phase 1 -- Hypertrophy/Joint Integrity (Weeks 1-2)**
- Slow eccentric movements, higher volume sets
- Focus on strength endurance and structural integrity
- 3 sessions per week
- Purpose: build tissue resilience, address imbalances

**Phase 2 -- Strength Block (Weeks 3-5)**
- 85-90% of 1RM
- Sets of 3-5 reps
- Focus on maximal strength development
- 2-3 sessions per week

**Phase 3 -- Power/Speed (Weeks 6-7)**
- Reduced volume, maximum intent
- Explosive movements, plyometrics
- 2 sessions per week

**Phase 4 -- Taper (Week 8)**
- Minimal S&C, maintain neural drive
- 1-2 brief sessions

**Variation Without Changing Exercises**
- Change tempo (slow eccentrics to explosive)
- Adjust rest periods
- Add accommodating resistance (bands/chains)
- This allows adaptation while maintaining movement proficiency

**Out-of-Camp Base Building**
- Higher training frequency (4-5x/week)
- More general preparation work
- Address weaknesses identified during camp
- Build work capacity for next camp

### MMA Application
- Exercises selected for transfer to fighting: rotational power, hip extension, grip strength, neck strength
- Hierarchy: skill training > tactical preparation > physical preparation (as fight approaches)
- Train fighters 2-3x/week during camp depending on experience level

### Notable Athletes
- Dustin Poirier, Joanna Jedrzejczyk, Colby Covington
- Won World MMA Awards Trainer of the Year 2019-20 and 2021

## 3. Firas Zahabi (Tristar Gym) -- Volume vs Intensity

### Key Training Principles
- **"The person who trains consistently at moderate intensity accumulates more volume over time than the person who goes all-out and needs days to recover."**
- Train at ~70% intensity most of the time rather than 100% three days a week
- Consistency beats intensity for skill acquisition and long-term development
- Never train so hard that you compromise tomorrow's session
- Stay in "flow state" -- if training is too intense, you leave flow and the session becomes counterproductive

### Specific Protocols

**Daily Training Approach**
- Train at 70-85% of maximum intensity
- Train MORE frequently (5-6x/week) rather than harder (3x/week to failure)
- Never be so sore you can't train the next day
- Accumulate more total training volume over weeks and months

**The "Day 2 Test"**
- If you can't train again the next day at a reasonable level, you went too hard
- Training should leave you feeling "pleasantly tired" not destroyed

**Sparring Philosophy**
- Light/technical sparring most of the time
- Hard sparring only occasionally and strategically
- Volume of quality reps > intensity of individual sessions

### MMA Application
- For fighters who train 2-3x/day (skills + S&C), this philosophy prevents overtraining
- Allows more mat time, which is where fights are won
- Reduces injury risk from chronic overtraining
- Fighter longevity -- sustainable training over a career

### Notable Concepts
- Made famous on Joe Rogan Experience MMA Show #32
- Flow state training: "Running full bore all the time harms your training because it pushes you out of flow state, making the workout unenjoyable, and exhausts you causing sore muscles, discouraging volume and consistency."
- This aligns with the Soviet/Eastern Bloc approach of high-frequency, sub-maximal training
- Georges St-Pierre trained under this philosophy for his career

## 4. Pavel Tsatsouline -- Strength Without Mass

### Key Training Principles
- **"Strength is a skill."** Strength is primarily a neural adaptation, not just a muscular one.
- Train frequently, never to failure, always leave reps in the tank
- Minimize muscle mass gain while maximizing strength -- critical for fighters who must make weight
- The nervous system can be trained to recruit more motor units and fire them faster without hypertrophy
- "Do not attempt a rep if there is any chance you might fail"

### Specific Protocols

**Power to the People (PttP) Protocol**
- Two exercises only: one pull (deadlift), one press (floor press or military press)
- 2 sets of 5 reps per exercise
- Set 1: work weight x 5
- Set 2: 90% of work weight x 5
- Train 5 days/week (Mon-Fri)
- Workout takes ~20-25 minutes
- Progression: add 5 lbs, hold for several sessions, add again
- Never grind reps -- every rep should look the same

**Grease the Groove (GTG)**
- Pick one exercise (pull-ups, push-ups, pistol squats, etc.)
- Do multiple sets throughout the day at 40-50% of your max reps
- Full recovery between sets (at least 15-30 minutes)
- Example: if max pull-ups = 10, do sets of 4-5 spread across the day
- Builds strength through neural pathway optimization without fatigue
- No soreness, no interference with other training

**Simple & Sinister (Kettlebell Protocol)**
- 100 one-arm kettlebell swings (sets of 10, alternating arms)
- 10 Turkish get-ups (alternating sides)
- Standards: Men -- 32kg "Simple," 48kg "Sinister"
- Daily practice, 30-40 minutes
- Builds total-body strength, hip power, shoulder stability, and anti-rotation

### MMA Application
- GTG is perfect for fighters: build pull-up/push-up/grip strength throughout the day without creating fatigue that interferes with skill sessions
- PttP builds a strong deadlift (hip extension power for takedowns, clinch work) and pressing strength with minimal time investment
- Simple & Sinister: kettlebell swings = hip hinge power (punching, takedown defense); Turkish get-ups = ground-to-standing transitions, shoulder stability for grappling
- All protocols designed to NOT produce significant muscle mass -- ideal for fighters making weight
- Can be layered alongside full MMA training schedule without recovery interference

### Notable Concepts
- "Strength is a skill" -- treat it like practicing a martial arts technique
- The concept of "neural strength" vs "muscular strength"
- Anti-glycolytic training: train power without producing excessive lactate

## 5. Alex Viada (Complete Human Performance) -- Concurrent Training

### Key Training Principles
- **MMA fighters are "obligate hybrid athletes"** -- they MUST perform across multiple energy systems simultaneously. They cannot choose to be only strong or only conditioned.
- You cannot simply stack a marathon plan on top of a powerlifting plan. Training must be integrated, not additive.
- Remove junk volume. Every session must have a clear goal.
- The interference effect is real but manageable with intelligent programming.
- Recovery cannot be overemphasized when combining strength and endurance work.

### Specific Protocols

**Programming Structure for Combat Athletes**
- Sparring and skill training are the PRIORITY DRIVERS across the week
- Consolidate other stressors (strength, power, endurance) to avoid overloading any single energy system
- Use the minimum effective volume for each quality
- 12-20 week templates with adjustable emphasis

**Managing the Interference Effect**
- Separate strength and endurance sessions by at least 6-8 hours when possible
- If doing same-day sessions, do the priority quality first
- Low-intensity aerobic work does NOT interfere with strength gains (and may enhance recovery)
- High-intensity interval work DOES compete with strength training for recovery resources
- Avoid high-volume hypertrophy work when endurance demands are high

**Prehabilitative Programming for Combat Athletes**
- Address anterior shoulder dominance (common in fighters)
- Prioritize posterior chain and rotator cuff work
- Include neck, grip, and wrist strength -- often forgotten but critical for combat sports
- Recovery sessions: easy runs, swims, or cycling at conversational pace

### MMA Application
- Fighters must be strong, powerful, AND have exceptional endurance -- the definition of hybrid
- Program design must account for the enormous training load from skill sessions (striking, grappling, sparring)
- S&C exists to support, not compete with, sport training
- Active recovery sessions (easy cardio) can enhance recovery between hard sessions

### Notable Concepts
- "Obligate hybrid athlete" concept for combat sports
- Viada himself squatted/deadlifted 700+ lbs while running sub-4:30 mile -- proof of concept for concurrent training
- The key insight: QUALITY over QUANTITY in every session

## 6. Mike Boyle -- Functional Strength & Single-Leg Emphasis

### Key Training Principles
- **Single-leg training is superior to bilateral squatting for athletes.** Most sport occurs on one leg; train accordingly.
- Core training should be ANTI-movement: anti-extension, anti-rotation, anti-lateral flexion
- Reduce spinal loading: single-leg work allows massive quad/glute development with 50% less weight on the spine
- If an exercise regularly hurts athletes, find a better alternative -- don't force the movement

### Specific Protocols

**Lower Body Programming**
- Primary movement: Rear-Foot Elevated Split Squat (RFESS) or single-leg squat variations
  - Loading: 78% front leg / 22% back leg (per force plate data)
  - Replace back squats entirely with these
  - NOTE: In 2025, Boyle moved away from RFESS because elite athletes were "gaming" the movement, shifting to other single-leg variations
- Hip hinge: trap bar deadlift, single-leg RDL
- Supplementary: sled pushes/pulls, lateral band walks, single-leg hip thrusts

**Core Training**
- Anti-extension: ab wheel rollouts, body saws, fallouts
- Anti-rotation: Pallof presses, single-arm carries, cable chops
- Anti-lateral flexion: suitcase carries, side planks
- NO sit-ups or crunches in his programming

**Upper Body**
- Horizontal push: push-up variations > bench press
- Horizontal pull: TRX rows, dumbbell rows (2:1 pull-to-push ratio minimum)
- Vertical push: landmine press, half-kneeling press
- Vertical pull: chin-ups, pull-ups

### MMA Application
- Single-leg strength directly transfers to shooting takedowns, defending takedowns, and kicking
- Anti-rotation core strength is essential for clinch work, scrambles, and maintaining posture in guard
- Reduced spinal loading means fighters can train heavy without the injury risk of heavy back squats
- Push-up variations translate directly to frames, posting, and ground-and-pound position

### Notable Concepts
- "New Functional Training for Sports" (book)
- The controversial stance against bilateral squatting -- but backed by decades of results with professional athletes
- Functional training = training movements and qualities that transfer to sport, not training muscles in isolation

## 7. Cal Dietz -- Triphasic Training for Combat Athletes

### Key Training Principles
- **Every athletic movement has three phases: eccentric (absorbing force), isometric (stabilizing), and concentric (producing force).** Train each separately for maximum development.
- Block periodization: dedicate focused blocks to each phase
- Supramaximal eccentric loading teaches the body to absorb and redirect forces beyond what it can concentrically produce
- Rate of force development (RFD) is more important than absolute strength for fighters

### Specific Protocols

**Triphasic Block Structure (adapted for MMA -- compressed 2-day model)**

**Eccentric Block (2 weeks)**
- 5-7 second eccentric lowering phase
- Medium day: 82.5-87.5% 1RM, singles/doubles
- High day: 90-97% 1RM, singles
- Low day: 75-80% 1RM, 3-4 reps
- Supramaximal eccentrics: 110-120% 1RM with slow lowering (requires spotters or safety racks)
- Purpose: teach muscles to absorb more force, increase tendon stiffness

**Isometric Block (2 weeks)**
- Pause at the most disadvantaged position for 3+ seconds
- Eliminates stretch reflex -- develops pure starting strength
- Same loading parameters as eccentric block but with isometric holds
- Purpose: build strength at sticking points, improve force production from static positions

**Concentric/Reactive Block (2 weeks)**
- Low reps, heavy weights initially
- Transitions to light weights with maximum speed
- French Contrast Method: heavy compound > plyometric > weighted jump > assisted jump
- Oscillatory reps: rapid partial reps to develop reflexive strength

**Application Schedule for MMA**
- Compressed blocks applied 3-5 times per year
- Usually 12-8 weeks out from fight
- 2-day per week model preferred over traditional 3-day
- De-load week between each phase transition
- Sustained strength and power levels throughout camp when properly peaked

### MMA Application
- Eccentric strength = absorbing strikes, deceleration, sprawling
- Isometric strength = holding positions in clinch, maintaining frames on the ground, grip fighting
- Concentric/reactive = explosive strikes, takedown entries, scramble speed
- French Contrast method develops the ability to produce force at multiple speeds -- exactly what fighting demands
- Supramaximal eccentrics build "body armor" -- the ability to absorb impact

### Notable Concepts
- "Triphasic Training" (book with Ben Peterson)
- The concept that most athletes only train the concentric phase and neglect 2/3 of the movement
- French Contrast training for developing the full force-velocity curve

## 8. Loren Landow -- Combat Athlete S&C

### Key Training Principles
- **"Maximize human performance through efficiency."** No wasted movements, no wasted training time.
- All biomotor skills must be developed: speed, power, strength, agility, flexibility (mobility + stability), balance, and conditioning
- Individual assessment before programming -- evaluate conditioning, strengths, and weaknesses
- Certified Muscle Activation Techniques (MAT) specialist -- uses soft tissue techniques to correct imbalances and enhance regeneration
- No one-size-fits-all approach

### Specific Protocols

**Program Structure (from "Ultimate Conditioning for Martial Arts")**
- 120 exercises catalogued, each targeting a specific martial arts attribute
- Programs organized by discipline:
  - Striking/kicking arts programs
  - Wrestling/grappling arts programs
  - Blended MMA programs

**Training Components**
- Dynamic warm-ups and flexibility protocols
- Base conditioning exercises
- Striking and kicking specific exercises (rotational power, core stiffness)
- Wrestling and grappling specific exercises (grip, pulling, hip mobility)
- Speed and agility work
- Recovery and nutrition protocols

**Periodization Approach**
- Training planned around fight schedule
- Physical qualities developed in priority order based on individual assessment
- Progressive overload with planned de-loads

### MMA Application
- Exercise selection driven by skill demands: what physical qualities does this fighter need most?
- Martial-arts-specific movement patterns integrated into S&C (not just general gym work)
- Recovery protocols integrated into programming (not an afterthought)
- Speed and agility development specific to ring/cage movement

### Notable Credentials
- 20+ years experience, 500+ professional athletes trained
- UFC, NFL, MLB, MLS, NHL, Olympic gold medalists
- S&C coach on The Ultimate Fighter Season 16
- Author: "Ultimate Conditioning for Martial Arts" (Human Kinetics)

## 9. Eric Cressey -- Mobility & Shoulder Health

### Key Training Principles
- **"Keep the ball on the socket."** Shoulder health is about maintaining proper glenohumeral joint mechanics under load and fatigue.
- Assessment-driven programming: identify whether limitations are mobility deficits, stability deficits, or both
- Look at the shoulder, thoracic spine, AND hips together -- they are interconnected
- More pulling than pushing, but only if pulling technique is correct
- Balance scapula-fixed exercises (bench press) with scapula-free exercises (push-ups, landmine press, cable work)

### Specific Protocols

**Shoulder Health Programming**
- 2:1 or 3:1 pull-to-push ratio in programming
- Include both scapula-fixed pressing (bench) and scapula-free pressing (push-ups, landmine)
- Rotator cuff strengthening: banded external rotations, side-lying external rotations, prone Y/T/W raises
- Thoracic spine mobility: foam roller extensions, side-lying windmills, open books
- Scapular stability: serratus anterior work (push-up plus), lower trap activation

**Assessment Framework**
- Check thoracic spine mobility
- Test shoulder internal/external rotation
- Assess scapular movement patterns
- Evaluate hip mobility (compensations travel up the chain)
- Different athletes may have healthy shoulders through different mechanisms -- assess and address individual weaknesses

**Concurrent Periodization**
- Uses concurrent periodization: trains multiple qualities simultaneously rather than sequential blocks
- Allows flexibility to adjust programming based on how each athlete responds
- Goal: get stronger while maintaining pain-free movement

### MMA Application
- Grapplers place enormous demands on shoulders: kimura defense, underhooks, framing, posting
- Anterior shoulder dominance is common in fighters (lots of punching, clinch work) -- must counter with posterior work
- Thoracic mobility affects ability to rotate for strikes AND to maintain posture in guard
- Hip mobility directly affects guard play, kicking, and takedown ability
- Push-up variations (scapula-free) are more shoulder-friendly than bench pressing for fighters

### Notable Concepts
- "Simplified Shoulder Solutions" (resource)
- The interconnection of thoracic spine, shoulder, and hip mobility
- Assessment-first approach: don't assume the problem, test for it

## 10. Joe DeFranco -- Westside for Skinny Bastards & Athlete GPP

### Key Training Principles
- **"The best way to get athletes faster and more explosive is to first get them stronger."**
- Adapted Westside Barbell's conjugated periodization for athletes (not just powerlifters)
- Template-based approach: choose exercises that carry over to YOUR sport, not rigidly prescribed movements
- Three methods of strength development: Max Effort, Dynamic Effort, Repetition Effort
- GPP (General Physical Preparedness) is the foundation upon which sport-specific work is built

### Specific Protocols

**WS4SB3 (Westside for Skinny Bastards Part III) -- 4-Day Template**

**Day 1 -- Max Effort Upper Body (Monday)**
- Main lift: bench press variant worked up to 3-5 RM
- Supplemental: horizontal pull (rows) 4x10-15
- Rear delt/rotator cuff work
- Elbow flexion (curls) 3x8-12
- Grip work

**Day 2 -- Dynamic Effort Lower Body (Tuesday)**
- Box jumps or broad jumps: 5-8 sets of 1-3 reps
- Unilateral movement: lunges, step-ups, or split squats 3x8-12
- Hip extension: glute-ham raise, pull-throughs 3x8-15
- Weighted abs

**Day 3 -- Repetition Upper Body (Thursday)**
- Main lift: DB bench, incline, or push-up variation for 3x20-30 OR 5x10
- Vertical pull: chin-ups or pull-ups 4xmax or 4x8-15
- Medial delt work
- Trap work (shrugs)
- Elbow flexion

**Day 4 -- Max Effort Lower Body (Friday)**
- Main lift: squat or deadlift variant worked up to 3-5 RM
- Unilateral movement 3x8-15
- Hip extension 3x8-15
- Weighted abs

**GPP/Conditioning (additional)**
- Sled drags: forward, backward, lateral
- Wheelbarrow walks
- Sledgehammer swings
- Tire flips
- Strongman-style carries
- Performed 2-3x/week on non-lifting days or after lifting

### MMA Application
- Max Effort work builds fight-ending power (the ability to produce maximum force)
- Dynamic Effort work develops speed-strength (fast hips for takedowns and strikes)
- Repetition work builds muscular endurance (ability to sustain output across rounds)
- GPP conditioning (sled work, carries) builds the general work capacity that supports fight camps
- Template flexibility: choose exercises that address YOUR weaknesses
- Trap bar deadlift emphasis -- DeFranco's fighters regularly pulling 500+ lbs with trap bar

### Notable Concepts
- Conjugated periodization adapted for athletes (not just raw powerlifters)
- Three methods address three different aspects of strength: max strength, speed, and endurance
- "The answers lie in the gym" -- test ideas with real athletes, not just theory

## 11. UFC Performance Institute -- Training Camp & Off-Camp Structure

### Overall Periodization Model

**Phase 1A -- Off-Camp General Preparation (no fight scheduled)**
- Duration: 1-3 blocks of 2-6 weeks each
- Non-specific, moderate intensity training
- Higher work volumes for "accumulation" of physical development
- Linear skill development with technical focus
- Build aerobic base, improve general strength, fix body composition
- Modalities: running, cycling, general strength training, mobility work
- Address injuries and weaknesses from previous camp

**Phase 1B -- Off-Camp Specific Preparation (fight confirmed, far out)**
- Transition toward more specific training
- Begin increasing intensity while maintaining volume
- Sport-specific conditioning begins to increase

**Phase 2 -- Fight Camp (fight scheduled)**
- Duration: 4 blocks of approximately 2 weeks each (8 weeks total)
- Concurrent loading of multiple physical qualities
- Fight-specific technique training at very high intensity
- Multi-focus, high-intensity, undulating volumes
- Daily undulating periodization to balance strength, power, and metabolic demands
- S&C sessions shaped around scheduled sparring rounds, injury history, and recent training load

**Phase 3 -- Late Camp / Taper (7-14 days out)**
- Progressive reduction in training volume
- Training intensity remains at fight intensity (very high)
- Training sessions reduced by 1/3
- Removal of heavy strength training
- No heavy training 2 days prior to competition
- Focus: sharpening, peaking, recovery

**Phase 4 -- Competition Week & Post-Fight**
- Minimal training, mental preparation
- Post-fight assessment
- Transition into restorative conditioning

### Assessment & Monitoring
- Force plate testing for strength/power profiling
- Motion capture systems for movement analysis
- Heart rate monitoring for conditioning assessment
- Profiles of how fighters respond to training, travel, and weight cuts
- Personalized programs built from data

### Key Principles
- Individualized progression prevents overtraining
- Daily undulating periodization is preferred over linear models for fight camp
- Physical preparation exists in service of skill/tactical development
- Recovery, medical support, and nutrition are integrated (not separate)

## Cross-Cutting Themes & Synthesis

### Universal Principles Across All Coaches

1. **Assessment Before Programming** -- Every coach emphasizes individual evaluation before prescribing training. No cookie-cutter programs.

2. **S&C Supports Skill Training** -- Physical preparation is the servant of technical/tactical development, not the master. Especially during fight camp.

3. **Aerobic Base is Foundational** -- Jamieson, Zahabi, Viada, and the UFC PI all emphasize building a strong aerobic base during off-camp periods.

4. **Reduce Volume as Fight Approaches** -- Universal taper: increase intensity, decrease volume, peak for fight night.

5. **Strength Without Unnecessary Mass** -- Pavel, Boyle, and DeFranco all provide methods for getting stronger without adding weight (critical for making weight class).

6. **Train Movements, Not Muscles** -- Functional patterns that transfer to fighting: hip hinge, single-leg, rotation, anti-rotation, push, pull.

7. **Recovery is Training** -- Every coach accounts for recovery as a programmed variable, not an afterthought.

8. **Prehab > Rehab** -- Cressey, Daru, Viada, and Landow all emphasize preventing injury through mobility work, balanced programming, and joint integrity.

### Recommended Integration for MMA Program Design

**Off-Camp (8-16 weeks, no fight scheduled)**
- Aerobic base building (Jamieson: cardiac output, 130-150 BPM, 30-60 min, 3-4x/week)
- Strength development (DeFranco WS4SB template or Dietz triphasic blocks, 3-4x/week)
- Mobility/prehab (Cressey shoulder protocols, hip mobility, thoracic spine work)
- Address weaknesses (Daru: hypertrophy/joint integrity phase)
- Higher S&C volume, moderate skill training volume
- GTG for bodyweight strength (Pavel: pull-ups, push-ups throughout day)

**Early Camp (8-6 weeks out)**
- Transition from general to specific conditioning (Jamieson: threshold training)
- Maintain/build max strength (Daru: 85-90% 1RM, sets of 3-5)
- Begin triphasic compressed blocks if using Dietz method
- 3x/week S&C, increasing skill training intensity
- Single-leg emphasis (Boyle: split squats, single-leg RDL)

**Mid Camp (6-3 weeks out)**
- Sport-specific conditioning (Jamieson: high-intensity intervals, alactic power)
- Power development (DeFranco: dynamic effort work)
- Concurrent training management (Viada: consolidate stressors, minimum effective volume)
- 2-3x/week S&C, high skill training volume/intensity
- Follow Zahabi principle: never so hard you can't train tomorrow

**Late Camp (3-1 weeks out)**
- Maintain neural drive, reduce volume (UFC PI: sessions reduced by 1/3)
- No heavy strength training in final 2 weeks
- High-intensity sharpening only
- 1-2x/week brief S&C sessions
- Focus on recovery, sleep, nutrition, weight management

**Fight Week**
- Minimal physical training
- Light movement, mobility, visualization
- No strength training 2 days prior to competition
