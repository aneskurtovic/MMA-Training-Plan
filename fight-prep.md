## Safety & Injury Prevention

> **Training through pain is not toughness — it is stupidity that costs you training weeks.** Every session missed to a preventable injury is a session your opponent is gaining on you.

### Concussion Protocol — Graduated Return-to-Play (6 Steps)

**Mandatory minimum stand-down: 7 days from the date of concussion.** No athlete may advance through these steps in less than 7 days total. Each step requires a minimum of 24 hours symptom-free before progressing to the next.

| Step | Activity | Objective | Duration |
|------|----------|-----------|----------|
| 1 | Complete physical and cognitive rest | Recovery | 24-48 hours minimum |
| 2 | Light aerobic exercise (walking, stationary bike at <70% max HR) | Increase heart rate | 1 day minimum |
| 3 | Sport-specific exercise (shadow boxing, movement drills — no contact, no head impact risk) | Add movement complexity | 1 day minimum |
| 4 | Non-contact training drills (pad work, grappling drills at 50%, technical sparring with zero contact to head) | Increase intensity, restore coordination | 1 day minimum |
| 5 | Full contact practice (return to normal sparring with trusted partners, coach monitoring) | Restore confidence, test functional recovery | 1 day minimum |
| 6 | Return to competition | Full clearance | Medical sign-off recommended |

- If symptoms return at any step, drop back to the previous step and wait 24 hours before retrying.
- A second concussion within 12 months requires medical clearance before returning to any contact training.
- **Fight camp context:** A concussion during camp that requires the full 7-day protocol will cost a minimum of 7-10 training days. If this occurs after Week 6, seriously evaluate whether the fight should proceed. Fighter health comes first.

### Emergency Red Flags — Go to the ER Immediately

| Red Flag | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Repeated vomiting | Two or more episodes after head impact |
| Seizure | Any seizure activity, even brief |
| Loss of consciousness >30 seconds | Unresponsive or unable to be woken |
| Worsening headache | Headache that intensifies over time rather than improving |
| Slurred speech | Difficulty forming words or sentences that weren't present before impact |
| Weakness or numbness | One-sided weakness, numbness in extremities, or difficulty with coordination |
| Unequal pupils | One pupil visibly larger than the other |
| Unusual drowsiness | Cannot stay awake, extreme confusion, or combativeness |

### Sparring Safety

#### Partner Matching

- Match sparring partners within **+/- 10% bodyweight**. For Anes at 77 kg (likely training around 80-82 kg), acceptable range is 72-90 kg.
- If a significant weight mismatch is unavoidable, the heavier partner must reduce power output proportionally.
- Skill level should be within 1-2 levels. Never pair a first-day beginner against an experienced fighter in live rounds.

#### Required Gear by Camp Phase

| Camp Phase | Required Gear |
|------------|---------------|
| Weeks 1-2 (base phase) | 16 oz gloves, mouthguard, cup |
| Weeks 3-8 (intensity phase) | All of the above + headgear mandatory, shin guards mandatory |
| Week 9 (fight week) | No sparring — technique only |

> **No exceptions on mouthguard and cup — ever.** Headgear is mandatory from Week 3 onward because sparring intensity increases to 85-90%. If headgear is not available, cap sparring intensity at 60%.

#### Corner Person Criteria

Every sparring session must have a designated corner person who:

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Experience | Must be an experienced fighter or coach who has cornered before |
| Authority | Has the authority to stop rounds at any time, for any reason |
| Monitoring | Actively watches for signs of fatigue, disorientation, or excessive damage |
| Positioning | Stands mat-side with a clear view of both fighters, not distracted |
| Communication | Can communicate clearly with both fighters and the timekeeper |

#### Post-Sparring Neuro Check

Perform after every sparring session, especially after any significant head contact:

| Check | Method | Concern If... |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| Orientation | Ask: "What day is it? Where are we? Who did you spar with?" | Cannot answer correctly or hesitates significantly |
| Balance | Stand on one foot for 10 seconds, eyes closed | Cannot hold balance, sways excessively, falls |
| Recall | Ask about a specific combination or exchange from the round | Cannot recall events from the session |
| Tracking | Follow a finger smoothly left to right, up and down | Eyes jump, cannot track smoothly, reports dizziness |

> **If the athlete fails any part of the neuro check:** no more training that day, monitor for 24 hours, apply the concussion protocol if symptoms persist.

### Injury Modification Table

| Body Part | Avoid | Substitutes | See Doctor If... |
|-----------|-------|-------------|------------------|
| **Shoulder** | Overhead pressing, bench press at full ROM, heavy pull-ups, throwing combinations with full extension | Landmine press (neutral path), floor press (limited ROM), band pull-aparts, isometric holds at pain-free angles | Pain at rest, inability to raise arm above shoulder height, clicking/catching with sharp pain, numbness down the arm |
| **Knee** | Deep squats, lunging with full depth, explosive jumping/landing, heavy leg press at full ROM | Box squat to pain-free depth, leg press at partial ROM, step-ups (low box), wall sits, hamstring curls, hip thrusts | Swelling that doesn't resolve in 48 hours, locking/giving way, inability to fully straighten or bend, pain walking downstairs |
| **Lower Back** | Heavy deadlifts, loaded spinal flexion (sit-ups, GHD), back squats with heavy load, explosive rotational movements | Hip hinge with light KB (RDL pattern), bird dogs, dead bugs, McGill Big 3 (curl-up, side plank, bird dog), belt squat | Pain radiating down either leg, numbness/tingling in feet, loss of bladder/bowel control, pain that worsens despite 5+ days rest |
| **Neck** | Neck bridges, heavy shrugs, sparring with head contact, any exercise that loads the neck under flexion | Isometric 4-way holds (light, pain-free), gentle ROM exercises, upper trap stretches, avoid headgear sparring until cleared | Pain radiating into arms/hands, persistent headache associated with neck movement, weakness in grip, dizziness when turning head |
| **Wrist/Hand** | Heavy gripping (deadlift, pull-ups, farmer's carry), wrist extension under load, heavy bag work without wraps | Straps for pulling movements, push-ups on fists or parallettes, hook grip alternatives, open-hand guard work, focus mitt work with proper wrapping | Swelling that doesn't resolve in 48 hours, inability to grip at all, visible deformity, numbness in fingers, pain on the snuffbox (scaphoid fracture risk) |
| **Ankle** | Running, box jumps, any plyometrics, heavy calf work, kicking with the injured side | Cycling or rowing for cardio, seated calf raises (light), balance board work at pain-free ROM, upper body and grappling focus | Inability to bear weight, significant bruising/swelling, pain on both sides of the ankle (possible fracture), instability (ankle gives way) |
| **Ribs** | Trunk rotation under load, heavy bag work, sparring (any contact), exercises requiring bracing under load (squats, deadlifts) | Machine-based isolation work (leg extension, hamstring curl, cable exercises), light technique drilling with no contact, walking/cycling for conditioning | Pain with deep breathing that worsens, bruising that spreads, pain on specific rib when pressed, shortness of breath |

#### General Injury Rules

1. If an exercise causes **sharp pain**, stop immediately. Dull discomfort during warm-up that resolves within 2-3 minutes is generally acceptable.
2. Never increase load on a modified exercise until the original exercise can be performed pain-free.
3. Ice acute injuries for 15-20 minutes, 3-4 times per day for the first 48-72 hours.
4. Log all injuries and modifications in your training journal — patterns reveal root causes.

### When to Stop Training / Pull Out of the Fight

| Situation | Decision |
|-----------|----------|
| Concussion after Week 6 | Strongly consider pulling out. A 2-3 week gap in sparring before a fight is dangerous. |
| Broken hand/foot | Pull out. Cannot compete safely with a fracture. |
| Knee or ankle ligament sprain (Grade 2+) | Pull out. Risk of catastrophic re-injury under fight conditions is too high. |
| Illness with fever within 2 weeks of fight | Reassess at 7 days out. If still symptomatic, pull out. |
| Weight cut not on track at Week 7 | If more than 5 kg above fight weight at start of Week 7, reassess viability. Cutting more than 8% bodyweight (>6.2 kg for a 77 kg class) is dangerous. |
| 3+ overtraining red flags for 5+ consecutive days | Mandatory deload. If within 2 weeks of fight, reduce volume dramatically but maintain skill work. |

## Conditioning Tests

### Testing Schedule

| Test Point | When | Purpose |
|------------|------|---------|
| **Baseline** | Week 0 (March 14-15, 2026) | Establish starting fitness levels, identify weaknesses, set training targets |
| **Mid-Camp** | Week 5 (April 18, during deload) | Measure progress, adjust programming for final 4 weeks |
| **Final** | Week 8 (May 9, 2026) | Confirm fight readiness, final confidence check |

> **Week 9 (fight week) has NO conditioning tests.** The body must be resting and recovering, not testing.

### Testing Conditions

- Same time of day for every test point
- Same equipment, same warm-up protocol, same test order
- Record date, time, fasted/fed status, sleep quality, and bodyweight
- If splitting across two days: cardiovascular + muscular endurance on Day 1, strength + MMA-specific on Day 2
- Allow at least one full rest day between testing and the next training session

### Warm-Up Protocol (Before Every Testing Session)

1. **5 min easy cardio** — row, bike, or jog at conversational pace
2. **Joint circles** — ankles, knees, hips, shoulders, wrists (10 each direction)
3. **Dynamic stretches** — leg swings (10/side), hip circles (10/side), arm circles (10/side), inchworms (5)
4. **Movement prep** — 5 bodyweight squats, 5 push-ups, 5 pull-apart band pulls
5. For strength tests, add 2-3 progressive warm-up sets (empty bar, 50%, 70%, 85%, then test attempt)

### Test Battery

**Rest between tests:** 3-5 minutes within a category, 5-10 minutes between categories.

### Epley Formula for 1RM Estimation

```
Estimated 1RM = Weight x (1 + Reps / 30)
```

Example: 3RM of 100 kg = 100 x (1 + 3/30) = 100 x 1.10 = **110 kg**

### Monitoring: HRV & Overtraining Indicators

#### Morning HRV Protocol

| Parameter | Protocol |
|-----------|----------|
| Timing | Measure immediately on waking, before getting out of bed |
| Position | Lying down (supine), relaxed, eyes closed |
| Duration | 60-second reading minimum |
| Equipment | Chest strap (preferred) or validated app with phone camera/wrist sensor |
| Recommended apps | Elite HRV, HRV4Training |
| Consistency | Same position, same time, same device every day |

#### Interpreting HRV Trends

Track the **7-day rolling average**, not individual daily readings.

| Trend | Interpretation | Action |
|-------|----------------|--------|
| 7-day avg stable or gradually rising | Good recovery, positive adaptation | Continue as planned |
| Single-day dip (returns next day) | Normal variation | No action needed |
| 3+ day downward trend | Functional overreaching — fatigue outpacing recovery | Reduce volume by 20%, prioritize sleep, check nutrition |
| 7-day avg drops >15% below baseline | Non-functional overreaching | **Immediate deload.** Reduce to recovery-only training. |
| Consistently suppressed for 2+ weeks despite deload | Possible overtraining syndrome | See a sports medicine professional. Training may need to stop entirely. |

#### Overtraining Red Flags Checklist

If **3 or more** of these are present simultaneously, adjust training immediately:

- [ ] Persistent fatigue despite adequate sleep (8+ hours)
- [ ] Resting heart rate elevated 5+ consecutive days above baseline
- [ ] Mood disturbances: irritability, apathy, loss of motivation to train
- [ ] Loss of appetite or unexplained weight loss
- [ ] Recurring illness (colds, infections) more than once per month
- [ ] Performance decline despite consistent training and recovery
- [ ] Difficulty sleeping despite fatigue (wired-but-tired feeling)
- [ ] Increased perceived effort on previously manageable loads (RPE 8 on what used to be RPE 6)

> **Overtraining is not a badge of honour — it is a programming failure.** Catching overreaching early via HRV trends and this checklist prevents it from becoming overtraining syndrome, which can take months to recover from.

### Scouting Framework

Begin opponent research as early as possible. The more data available, the better the game plan.

#### Information Gathering

| Source | What to Look For | Priority |
|--------|-----------------|----------|
| **Fight footage** (minimum 3 bouts) | Stance, preferred range, go-to techniques, habits under pressure, cardio fade, recovery patterns | Critical |

## Mental Preparation

### Visualization Protocols

Visualization is a skill. It must be trained consistently to be effective on fight night.

#### Daily Visualization Schedule

| Camp Phase | Frequency | Duration | Focus |
|------------|-----------|----------|-------|
| Weeks 1-4 | 1x daily (evening) | 5-10 min | General — see yourself training hard, executing techniques well |
| Weeks 5-7 | 1x daily (evening) | 10-15 min | Specific — visualize the opponent, your game plan sequences, specific exchanges |
| Weeks 8-9 | 2x daily (morning + evening) | 10-15 min each | Fight simulation — walk through the entire fight night from warm-up to hand raised |

#### Visualization Script — Fight Night (Use from Week 7 Onward)

Walk through each of these scenes in vivid, multi-sensory detail. See it, hear it, feel it.

1. **Arrival at the venue.** Feel the energy of the building. You are calm and prepared.
2. **Locker room warm-up.** Pads with your coach. Body feels loose and sharp. Hands are fast.
3. **The walk out.** Music playing. Crowd noise. You are focused. Breathing is controlled.
4. **Entering the cage.** Touch the canvas. Bounce on your feet. The cage feels like home.
5. **Referee instructions.** Touch gloves. Return to your corner. Deep breath.
6. **Round 1 opening 30 seconds.** Execute your opening sequence. Find your range. Establish your jab.
7. **A moment of adversity.** The opponent lands a hard shot. You eat it, reset, and respond with your trained combination. You are not shaken.
8. **Executing your game plan.** See yourself landing your best techniques, implementing takedowns or sprawls as planned, controlling the pace.
9. **The final round.** You are tired but your conditioning carries you. You push the pace. The opponent is fading.
10. **The final bell.** You know you have done enough. Hand raised.

#### Key Principles

- Always visualize in the **first person** (through your own eyes, not watching yourself from outside).
- Include **all senses**: the sound of the crowd, the feel of the canvas under your feet, the smell of the venue, the taste of your mouthguard.
- **Visualize adversity and recovery**, not just success. Seeing yourself overcome difficulty builds genuine confidence.
- If your mind wanders, gently return to the scene. Do not force it.

### Pre-Fight Routine

Establish a consistent pre-fight routine during camp and rehearse it before every hard sparring session so it becomes automatic.

#### 2 Hours Before Fight

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| T-120 min | Arrive at venue. Find the locker room. Settle in. |
| T-90 min | Light movement — walking, dynamic stretching, joint circles. Nothing intense. |
| T-60 min | Mental preparation — 10 minutes of visualization, controlled breathing (box breathing: 4 in, 4 hold, 4 out, 4 hold). |
| T-45 min | Begin physical warm-up with coach. Shadow boxing (3 rounds, light), movement patterns, footwork drills. |
| T-30 min | Pad work with coach (3-4 rounds, building from 50% to 80% intensity). Focus on the opening sequences of the game plan. |
| T-15 min | Glove up. Final pad round at fight pace. Short burst of high intensity to activate the nervous system. |
| T-10 min | Hands wrapped, gloves on. Sit quietly. Controlled breathing. Trust your preparation. |
| T-5 min | Stand up. Bounce lightly. Coach gives final cues — short, simple, rehearsed. |
| Walk out | Go. |

### Weight Cut Mental Management

The weight cut is the most mentally challenging part of fight camp. It must be managed deliberately.

| Phase | Mental Strategy |
|-------|----------------|
| **Weeks 1-6 (gradual cut)** | Focus on process, not the scale. Daily weigh-ins are data, not judgments. If weight is trending correctly, trust the process. |
| **Week 7-8 (intensified cut)** | Expect irritability, low energy, and mood swings. **These are physiological, not psychological.** Warn family and training partners in advance. Reduce decision-making outside of training. Simplify your life. |
| **Week 9 (water cut, if needed)** | This is the hardest 24-48 hours. Have a precise plan with exact times, amounts, and methods. Do not improvise. Conserve all energy for the weigh-in and rehydration. |
| **After weigh-in** | The relief is immediate. Follow the rehydration plan precisely. Do not overeat. The discipline continues until after the fight. |

#### Coping Strategies During the Cut

- **Structured distraction:** Have a playlist, podcasts, or shows ready for the final water-cut hours. Keep the mind occupied.
- **Countdown mentality:** "X hours until weigh-in" is more manageable than thinking about how you feel right now.
- **Partner accountability:** Have someone with you during the final cut who knows the plan and keeps you on track.
- **Reframe suffering:** Every fighter on the card is going through the same thing. This is part of the job.

## Fight Day Protocol

### Morning of Weigh-In (Friday, May 15)

| Time | Action |
|------|--------|
| Wake (6:00 AM) | Weigh yourself on your own scale immediately. Know exactly where you stand. |
| 6:00-7:00 AM | If on weight: relax, sip water (small amounts). If over: execute the final cut protocol (hot bath/sauna — **never alone**). |
| 8:00-9:00 AM | Travel to weigh-in venue. Bring a towel, change of clothes, and your rehydration supplies. |
| Weigh-in | Step on the scale. Make weight. Done. |
| Immediately after | Begin rehydration protocol (see below). |

### Rehydration Plan (Post Weigh-In)

> **The goal is to restore bodyweight to approximately 82-84 kg (6-9% above fight weight) by fight time.** This must be done gradually and systematically, not by gorging.

#### Phase 1: First 2 Hours Post Weigh-In

| What | Amount | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| Oral rehydration solution (ORS) | 500 ml immediately, then sip 500 ml over next hour | Pedialyte, WHO ORS, or similar. NOT plain water — you need electrolytes. |
| Light carbohydrate snack | 200-300 cal | White rice, banana, white bread with honey. Easy to digest. No fiber, no fat. |
| Avoid | Large meals, carbonated drinks, dairy, high-fiber foods, caffeine | The gut is compromised from the cut. Ease it back. |

#### Phase 2: 2-8 Hours Post Weigh-In

| What | Amount | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| Continue sipping ORS or electrolyte drink | 250 ml every 30 minutes | Total target: 2-3 liters in this phase |
| Meal 1 (2-3 hours post weigh-in) | 500-700 cal | White rice + lean protein (chicken, fish) + small amount of salt. No heavy sauces. |
| Meal 2 (5-6 hours post weigh-in) | 500-700 cal | Similar to Meal 1. Add a small amount of healthy fat (avocado, olive oil). |
| Weigh yourself | After Meal 2 | You should be 79-81 kg at this point. If not, increase fluid intake slightly. |

#### Phase 3: Evening / Before Bed

### Warm-Up Sequence (Pre-Fight)

This should take approximately 30-35 minutes, starting 45 minutes before expected walk-out.

| Phase | Duration | Activity |

| 2. Movement | 5 min | Shadow boxing — light, focused on footwork and distance. Not power. |

### Corner Communication Plan

Establish these during camp and use them in every sparring session so they are automatic on fight night.

#### Verbal Cues

| Cue | Meaning |
|-----|---------|
| "Jab" | Re-establish the jab. You're being too passive. |
| "Breathe" | You're tensing up. Relax, reset, exhale on strikes. |
| "Levels" | Switch to body attacks. The head is too well-defended. |
| "Hands up" | Guard has dropped. Protect yourself. |
| "Push" | Increase pace. You need this round. Outwork the opponent. |
| "Time" | Settle down. You are ahead. Control the pace, don't take risks. |
| "Underhook" | Get the underhook in the clinch. You're losing position. |
| "Off the cage" | You're stuck on the fence. Circle out, create space. |
| "Plan B" | Switch to the backup strategy. Plan A is not working. |
| "Finish" | Opponent is hurt. Swarm intelligently — don't get wild. |

#### Between-Round Protocol

| Time | Corner Action |
|------|---------------|
| 0-15 sec | Sit the fighter down. Water. Ice on neck if needed. Say nothing yet — let him breathe. |
| 15-30 sec | **One tactical adjustment.** Maximum two sentences. "He's dropping his right hand after the jab. Double jab, straight right." |
| 30-45 sec | **One emotional cue.** "You're ahead. Stay calm." or "You need this round. Push the pace." |
| 45-55 sec | Mouthguard in. Stand up. "You're ready. Go." |
| 55-60 sec | Fighter standing, bouncing, focused. No more talking. |

> **The corner must resist the urge to give too much information between rounds.** A fighter who is fatigued and full of adrenaline can process ONE instruction, maybe two. More than that creates confusion. Pick the single most important adjustment and deliver it clearly.
