# Exercise Glossary

This glossary covers every exercise used in the MMA training program. Each entry explains setup, execution, common errors, and — most importantly — how the movement transfers to fighting. Use this as a quick reference when programming or coaching.

---

## 1. Compound Barbell Lifts

| Exercise | Primary Muscles | Equipment | Setup | Execution Cues | Common Mistakes | MMA Benefit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Back Squat | Quads, glutes, adductors, erectors | Barbell, squat rack | Bar on upper traps (high bar) or rear delts (low bar). Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out. | "Brace hard, spread the floor, hips and knees break together. Drive up through mid-foot." | Knees caving, forward lean / chest drop, cutting depth short, losing brace at the bottom. | Builds the lower-body force production that powers level changes, takedown defense (standing up under pressure), and knockout-level punching from the legs up. |
| Front Squat | Quads, glutes, upper back, core | Barbell, squat rack | Bar in front rack (clean grip or cross-arm). Elbows high, chest tall. Feet shoulder-width. | "Elbows up the whole way. Sit straight down between your hips. Drive up keeping torso vertical." | Elbows dropping (bar rolls forward), wrist inflexibility forcing cross-arm when clean grip is better, shallow depth. | Upright torso pattern mimics posture needed in the clinch and during double-leg finishes. Heavy anterior core demand transfers to resisting being bent over in grappling. |
| Deadlift | Posterior chain (glutes, hamstrings, erectors), lats, grip | Barbell | Feet hip-width under bar, shins near bar. Hinge at hips, grip just outside knees. Neutral spine, lats engaged. | "Push the floor away, lock lats, bar drags up the legs. Stand tall — squeeze glutes at lockout." | Rounding the lower back, jerking the bar off the floor, hips shooting up first, hyperextending at lockout. | Total-body posterior chain strength for explosive hip extension — the engine behind throws, slams, sprawls, and heavy rear-hand strikes. Grip endurance under load. |
| Romanian Deadlift (RDL) | Hamstrings, glutes, erectors | Barbell | Stand with bar at hip height (from rack or after a deadlift). Feet hip-width, slight knee bend, shoulders packed. | "Push hips straight back like closing a car door. Bar stays on the thighs/shins. Feel a deep hamstring stretch, then drive hips forward." | Bending the knees too much (turns into a squat), rounding the back, not hinging far enough. | Eccentric hamstring strength reduces knee-injury risk from sprawling and kicking. Hip-hinge patterning powers underhooks-to-trips and mat returns. |
| Bench Press | Pecs, anterior delts, triceps | Barbell, bench, rack | Lie on bench, eyes under bar. Retract and depress shoulder blades. Grip slightly wider than shoulder-width. Feet flat on floor. | "Unrack, control bar to lower chest, drive through feet, press bar in a slight arc back over shoulders." | Flaring elbows to 90 degrees (shoulder stress), bouncing bar off chest, losing scapular retraction, lifting hips. | Horizontal pressing power for frames, stiff-arms, and bench pressing an opponent off you from bottom position. Tricep strength aids in posting and creating distance. |
| Overhead Press | Deltoids, triceps, upper traps, core | Barbell, squat rack | Bar in front rack at collarbone. Feet hip-width, glutes and abs braced. | "Press bar straight up — move your head out of the way, then back through once bar passes. Lock out directly over mid-foot." | Excessive back arch (turns into incline press), pressing the bar forward instead of straight up, not bracing core. | Overhead strength for posting on the cage, hand fighting from collar ties, and resisting being driven down in the clinch. Core stability under overhead load mirrors scramble demands. |
| Barbell Row | Lats, rhomboids, rear delts, biceps, erectors | Barbell | Hinge to roughly 45-degree torso. Overhand or underhand grip just outside knees. Slight knee bend. | "Pull bar to lower sternum, squeeze shoulder blades at top, control the negative. Torso stays still." | Excessive torso movement (rowing with momentum), pulling to the belly, standing too upright. | Pulling strength for clinch work, underhook control, arm drags, and guillotine finishes. Maintains upper-back posture to resist being broken down. |
| Hang Clean | Quads, glutes, traps, upper back, forearms | Barbell | Start standing with bar at hip height, overhand grip. Hinge slightly to bring bar to just above the knee (hang position). | "Explode hips, shrug, pull elbows high, catch in front rack with a quarter squat. Stand up." | Arm-pulling instead of hip-driving, not getting elbows through fast enough, catching on wrists instead of delts. | Triple extension (ankle-knee-hip) is the same motor pattern as shooting a double-leg or throwing an uppercut. Teaches rate of force development — how fast you can express strength. |
| Push Press | Deltoids, triceps, quads, glutes, core | Barbell, squat rack | Bar in front rack at collarbone. Feet hip-width. | "Quick dip (4-6 inches) keeping torso vertical, then drive aggressively through legs and press bar overhead in one motion. Lock out." | Dipping forward (weight on toes), pressing before legs finish driving, slow dip killing the stretch-shortening cycle. | Teaches legs-to-arms force transfer — the same kinetic chain used in every punch and in off-balancing an opponent from the clinch. Develops overhead lockout power for cage work. |

## 2. Single-Leg / Unilateral

| Exercise | Primary Muscles | Equipment | Setup | Execution Cues | Common Mistakes | MMA Benefit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| RFESS (Bulgarian Split Squat) | Quads, glutes, adductors | Dumbbells or barbell, bench | Rear foot laces-down on bench behind you. Front foot far enough forward that knee stays over ankle at bottom. | "Lower straight down until rear knee nearly touches floor. Drive through front heel. Keep torso upright." | Front foot too close to bench (knee shoots forward), torso leaning excessively, rear foot bearing too much load. | Single-leg strength and balance for kicking, sprawling off one leg, and maintaining base when an opponent grabs a leg. Reduces bilateral strength imbalances that cause injury. |
| Single-Leg RDL | Hamstrings, glutes, core (anti-rotation) | Dumbbell or kettlebell | Stand on one leg, soft knee. Hold weight in opposite hand. | "Hinge at the hip, free leg extends behind you as a counterbalance. Back stays flat. Squeeze glute to stand." | Rounding the back, opening the hip (rotating), not hinging deep enough, rushing the movement. | Posterior chain strength on one leg directly transfers to kicking (standing leg stability), single-leg takedown defense, and landing after throwing knees. Anti-rotation demand mirrors the rotational chaos of grappling. |
| Walking Lunges | Quads, glutes, hip flexors | Dumbbells, barbell, or bodyweight | Stand tall, weights at sides or on back. | "Take a long step forward, lower rear knee toward floor, drive through front heel to step into the next rep. Stay tall." | Short steps (knee over toe), torso collapsing forward, pushing off rear foot instead of pulling with front leg. | Dynamic single-leg strength with a locomotion component — transfers to shooting entries, level changes, and maintaining balance while moving forward in the pocket. |
| Step-Ups | Quads, glutes | Box (16-20 inches), dumbbells | Place one foot fully on the box. Hold dumbbells at sides. | "Drive through the top foot to stand on the box. Do NOT push off the bottom foot. Control the descent." | Using the bottom foot to push off (cheating), box too low to challenge the movement, leaning forward. | Concentric single-leg power for driving off the mat (standing up from bottom), penetration steps for takedowns, and explosive level changes. |
| Pistol Squat Progression | Quads, glutes, hip flexors, ankles | Bodyweight (band or box for assistance) | Stand on one leg. Extend the other leg in front of you. Arms forward for counterbalance. | "Sit back and down on one leg, keeping heel flat. Go as low as mobility allows, then drive up. Use a band or box to assisted versions as needed." | Knee collapsing inward, heel lifting, rounding the lower back at the bottom, using momentum to stand. | Ultimate expression of single-leg strength and mobility. Translates to recovering balance after being swept or pushed, deep single-leg positions in scrambles, and demonstrating the mobility fighters need at the hips and ankles. |

## 3. Power / Explosive

| Exercise | Primary Muscles | Equipment | Setup | Execution Cues | Common Mistakes | MMA Benefit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Box Jumps | Quads, glutes, calves | Plyo box (20-30 inches) | Stand facing box, feet shoulder-width, arms back. | "Swing arms, extend hips-knees-ankles, land softly on box with full foot contact. Stand tall, step down." | Rebounding off the floor (use a reset between reps), landing in a deep squat on the box (means the box is too high), jumping down instead of stepping. | Trains rate of force development and reactive power for explosive takedown entries, sprawls, and closing distance to land strikes. |
| Broad Jumps | Glutes, quads, hamstrings, calves | Open floor space | Feet shoulder-width, arms loaded behind you. | "Swing arms forward, drive hips and jump forward for max distance. Absorb landing with soft knees, stick it." | Not using the arms, landing stiff-legged, not committing to full hip extension. | Horizontal explosive power — the exact vector used in shooting for takedowns and closing distance for punches. Teaches aggressive hip extension into a target. |
| Med Ball Overhead Slam | Lats, core, shoulders, triceps | Slam ball (15-30 lbs) | Stand tall, ball overhead with arms extended. | "Brace core, slam ball into the ground as hard as possible using your whole body. Hinge and follow through. Catch the bounce or pick it up." | Using only arms (not engaging core/hips), not following through, too light a ball. | Full-body power expression similar to throwing hammerfists from top position or slamming an opponent in a takedown finish. Teaches aggressive downward force production. |
| Med Ball Rotational Slam | Obliques, core, hips, shoulders | Slam ball (10-20 lbs), wall | Stand perpendicular to wall, ball at hip opposite the wall. Feet slightly wider than shoulder-width. | "Rotate hips first, then trunk, then arms — slam ball into wall. Catch and reset. Load from the back hip." | Arm-throwing instead of hip-rotating, feet staying flat (rotate on the ball of the back foot), standing too close to the wall. | Directly trains the rotational power sequence of hooks, overhands, and round kicks. Hip-first rotation is the key to fight-ending power in strikes. |
| Med Ball Chest Pass | Pecs, triceps, anterior delts, core | Med ball (10-20 lbs), wall or partner | Stand facing wall, ball at chest, elbows in. | "Drive ball off chest explosively into wall, extending arms fully. Catch and immediately redirect. Stay on the balls of your feet." | Stepping forward (losing base), lobbing instead of driving, slow catch-and-redirect. | Trains horizontal pushing speed for frames, posting, and bench-pressing an opponent off you. Reactive catch-and-redirect mirrors the speed demands of hand fighting. |
| KB Swings | Glutes, hamstrings, core, lats | Kettlebell | Feet slightly wider than shoulder-width. KB on floor a foot in front of you. Hike the bell back between legs. | "Snap hips forward — the arms just go along for the ride. Bell should float to chest/eye height (Russian) or overhead (American). Squeeze glutes hard at the top." | Squatting the swing (should be a hinge), using the arms to lift, hyperextending the back at the top. | Powerful hip snap transfers directly to striking power, sprawl speed, and bridging/shrimping in grappling. High-rep swings also build posterior chain endurance for later rounds. |
| KB Snatch | Glutes, hamstrings, shoulders, traps, core | Kettlebell | Similar to swing start. One hand on the bell. | "Hike back, snap hips, as the bell rises guide it around your wrist and punch through to lockout overhead. Control the descent by redirecting the bell back between your legs." | Bell banging the wrist (path is wrong), pressing out the top instead of punching through, not using the hips. | Combines explosive hip extension with overhead stability and grip — mirrors the full-body power expression of throwing an overhand right or finishing a high-crotch lift. Excellent conditioning effect. |
| Hang Clean | — | — | — | — | — | See Compound Barbell Lifts section above. |
| Push Press | — | — | — | — | — | See Compound Barbell Lifts section above. |
| Battle Rope Slams | Shoulders, core, lats, grip | Battle ropes (1.5-2 inch, 40-50 ft) | Stand in athletic stance, slight hinge, one rope end in each hand. Anchor point ahead. | "Alternate arms (waves) or slam both together. Generate force from the hips, not just the shoulders. Keep a rhythm — don't let the ropes go slack." | Standing too upright (no hip involvement), death-gripping (forearms burn out instantly), standing too close to the anchor (no tension). | Upper-body power-endurance under fatigue — simulates the repeated explosive arm actions of punching in later rounds. Bilateral slams train ground-and-pound power; alternating waves build the shoulder endurance needed for long clinch exchanges. |

## 4. Upper Body Accessories

| Exercise | Primary Muscles | Equipment | Setup | Execution Cues | Common Mistakes | MMA Benefit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Weighted Pull-Ups | Lats, biceps, rear delts, grip | Pull-up bar, dip belt or dumbbell between feet | Hang from bar, overhand grip slightly wider than shoulders. Add load via belt or dumbbell. | "Initiate with lats (think elbows to hips), pull chin over bar, control the descent to full extension." | Kipping / swinging, not reaching full extension at the bottom, chin not clearing the bar. | Pulling strength is essential for clinch control, guillotines, front headlocks, arm drags, and dragging an opponent down from the cage. Grip under load builds lasting clinch endurance. |
| Dumbbell Row | Lats, rhomboids, rear delts, biceps | Dumbbell, bench | One knee and hand on bench, opposite foot on floor. Flat back, dumbbell hanging at arm's length. | "Pull dumbbell to hip, squeezing shoulder blade back and down. Lower under control." | Rotating the torso to heave the weight, rowing to the shoulder instead of the hip, rushing reps. | Unilateral pulling strength for single-collar ties, underhook battles, and controlling an arm in guard. Addresses left-right imbalances common in fighters with a dominant stance. |
| Landmine Press | Chest, shoulders, triceps, core (anti-rotation) | Barbell, landmine attachment (or corner) | Stand in split stance, hold the end of the barbell at shoulder height with one hand. | "Press the bar up and forward at a slight angle. Squeeze the core to prevent rotation. Control the return." | Flaring the elbow, rotating the torso, not bracing the core, pressing straight up instead of along the bar's arc. | The angled pressing path closely mimics a jab and cross. Single-arm execution demands anti-rotation — the same core stability needed when posting off an opponent or framing from bottom. |
| Incline DB Press | Upper pecs, anterior delts, triceps | Dumbbells, adjustable bench (30-45 degrees) | Sit on incline bench, dumbbells at shoulders, feet flat on floor, back pressed into pad. | "Press dumbbells up and slightly inward, without clanking them at the top. Lower to shoulders with control." | Over-arching the back (reducing the incline angle), flaring elbows, bouncing at the bottom. | Upper chest and shoulder pressing strength for frames from bottom, posting on the cage, and pushing an opponent away. Dumbbell path allows shoulder-friendly range of motion for fighters with existing shoulder wear. |
| Push-Up — Standard | Pecs, triceps, anterior delts, core | Bodyweight | Hands shoulder-width, body in a straight line from head to heels. | "Lower chest to the floor, elbows at 45 degrees, press back up. Lock out at top." | Sagging hips, flaring elbows to 90 degrees, partial range of motion. | Foundational pressing endurance for maintaining frames, escaping bottom positions, and posting. High-rep capacity means pressing strength that lasts into championship rounds. |
| Push-Up — Diamond | Triceps, inner chest, core | Bodyweight | Hands together under chest forming a diamond shape with index fingers and thumbs. | "Keep elbows tight to the body as you lower. Touch chest to hands, press up." | Flaring elbows, letting hips sag, reducing range of motion. | Tricep-dominant pressing for short-range pushing — creating frames inside the guard and posting off an opponent's head in scrambles. |
| Push-Up — Archer | Chest, triceps, core | Bodyweight | Wide hand placement. One arm bends while the other stays extended to the side. | "Lower toward one hand, keeping the other arm straight as a guide. Push back up. Alternate sides." | Rotating the torso, bending the straight arm, shortening the range. | Asymmetric pressing strength for single-arm frames and posts — common in scrambles where only one arm is available for base. Bridges the gap toward one-arm push-up strength. |
| Push-Up — Clap | Pecs, triceps, fast-twitch fibers | Bodyweight | Standard push-up position. | "Explode off the floor, clap, land with soft elbows, immediately descend into the next rep." | Landing with locked elbows (injury risk), losing body alignment, not achieving full flight. | Upper-body plyometric power for explosive frames and the initial burst of a punch. Trains the stretch-shortening cycle in horizontal pressing — speed kills in striking. |
| Dips | Triceps, lower chest, anterior delts | Parallel bars or rings | Grip the bars, arms locked, slight forward lean. | "Lower until upper arms are parallel to floor, press back up to full lockout. Keep elbows tracking over wrists." | Going too deep (shoulder stress), excessive forward lean, partial range of motion. | Pressing strength from a depressed shoulder position — directly transfers to posting on the mat, pressing up from bottom, and generating pushing force when pinned against the cage. |
| Face Pulls | Rear delts, external rotators, mid traps | Cable machine or band | Rope attachment at face height. Step back to create tension. | "Pull rope to face, splitting hands apart and externally rotating at the end. Squeeze for a beat. Slow return." | Pulling too low (becomes a row), using body momentum, not externally rotating at the end position. | Shoulder health and postural balance. Fighters do enormous volumes of pressing and internal rotation (punching, pushing); face pulls counterbalance this to keep shoulders healthy and maintain posture against collar ties. |
| Band Pull-Aparts | Rear delts, rhomboids, mid traps | Resistance band | Hold band at arm's length in front of chest, hands shoulder-width, palms down. | "Pull band apart by squeezing shoulder blades together until band touches chest. Slow return." | Shrugging shoulders, bending elbows, using momentum. | Scapular health and posture maintenance. Keeps the upper back strong and shoulders balanced against the high volume of punching and reaching in training. Ideal daily-dose prehab movement. |

## 5. Core / Anti-Movement

| Exercise | Primary Muscles | Equipment | Setup | Execution Cues | Common Mistakes | MMA Benefit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pallof Press | Obliques, transverse abdominis, hip stabilizers | Cable machine or band | Stand perpendicular to cable, handle at chest. Feet shoulder-width, slight athletic bend. | "Press handle straight out from chest and hold. Resist the rotation. Return to chest. Keep hips square." | Letting the cable rotate you, locking knees, pressing at an angle instead of straight out. | Anti-rotation core stability — the ability to resist being twisted or turned in the clinch, during scrambles, and when absorbing body shots. Directly trains the core's primary fight function: resisting unwanted movement. |
| Ab Wheel Rollout | Rectus abdominis, obliques, lats, hip flexors | Ab wheel | Kneel on a pad, grip the wheel, arms extended. | "Roll forward slowly, keeping hips extended (don't sit back). Go as far as you can control, then pull back using your abs." | Sagging the lower back, bending at the hips instead of extending, going farther than you can control. | Anti-extension core strength for maintaining posture in the clinch, resisting back bends in wrestling, and stabilizing the spine during strikes. The eccentric challenge mirrors being pulled forward by an opponent. |
| Hanging Leg Raises | Hip flexors, lower abs, grip | Pull-up bar | Hang from bar with full grip, shoulders packed. | "Raise legs to parallel (or toes to bar for advanced). Control the descent — no swinging. Initiate from the core, not momentum." | Swinging, using momentum, only lifting knees (if full leg raises are the target), losing grip. | Hip flexor strength for guard retention, high kicks, and knee strikes. Grip endurance while performing a core movement mirrors the simultaneous demands of hanging on an opponent while working your core in the clinch. |
| Copenhagen Plank | Adductors, obliques, core | Bench | Side-lying, top foot on bench, bottom foot hanging. Support on forearm. | "Lift hips and bottom leg so the body forms a straight line. Hold. The top leg's adductor does the heavy lifting." | Rotating hips forward or back, sagging at the hip, holding breath. | Adductor and lateral core strength critical for closed guard, triangle setups, body lock squeezes, and injury prevention during lateral movement and kicking. Groin injuries are common in MMA — this is direct armor. |
| Dead Bug | Deep core stabilizers, hip flexors | Bodyweight (mat) | Lie on back, arms reaching to ceiling, knees bent at 90 degrees over hips. Press low back into floor. | "Extend opposite arm and leg toward the floor while keeping the low back glued to the mat. Return and switch sides." | Low back arching off the floor, moving too fast, not exhaling fully on extension. | Teaches the core to stabilize the spine while the limbs move independently — exactly what happens when striking, grappling, and transitioning between positions. Foundation for all other core work. |
| Bird Dog | Erectors, glutes, deep core stabilizers | Bodyweight (mat) | Hands-and-knees position, wrists under shoulders, knees under hips. | "Extend opposite arm and leg until parallel with the floor. Pause. Return. Keep hips level — no rotation." | Rotating the hips, arching the back, rushing through reps, lifting the limbs too high. | Anti-rotation and anti-extension in a quadruped position — directly mimics the demands of maintaining base in turtle position and during scrambles on the mat. Excellent warm-up and prehab movement. |
| Farmer's Carry | Traps, grip, core (anti-lateral flexion), whole body | Heavy dumbbells or farmer's walk handles | Stand tall with heavy weights at your sides. Shoulders packed, chest up. | "Walk with short, controlled steps. Stay tall — don't lean. Squeeze the handles hard. Breathe." | Leaning to one side, taking long strides, shrugging the shoulders, letting posture break down. | Total-body stability, grip endurance, and postural strength under load. Directly transfers to clinch endurance, maintaining posture while gripping an opponent, and the general full-body tension required in grappling. |
| Suitcase Carry | Obliques, core (anti-lateral flexion), grip | Heavy dumbbell or kettlebell | Hold a heavy weight in one hand at your side. Stand tall, don't lean toward or away from the load. | "Walk with controlled steps, resisting the urge to lean toward the loaded side. Stay perfectly upright." | Leaning toward the weight, compensating with the opposite hip, shrugging the loaded shoulder. | Unilateral anti-lateral flexion — the ability to resist being pulled to one side. Transfers to single-collar tie defense, resisting an arm drag, and maintaining posture when an opponent pulls one side of your body. |
| Turkish Get-Up | Shoulders, core, hips, stabilizers (full body) | Kettlebell | Lie on back, press kettlebell up with one arm. Opposite arm and leg angled out for base. | "Roll to elbow, post on hand, bridge hips, sweep leg under to kneeling, stand up. Reverse the sequence. Eyes on the bell at all times." | Rushing the movement, losing visual contact with the bell, skipping the hip bridge, pressing the bell instead of keeping the arm vertical. | The most MMA-specific core exercise. Every phase mimics a grappling transition: bridging from bottom, posting up to an elbow, creating a frame, building base, and standing from the mat — all while stabilizing load overhead (simulating controlling an opponent's limb). |

## 6. Prehab / Mobility

| Exercise | Primary Muscles | Equipment | Setup | Execution Cues | Common Mistakes | MMA Benefit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Band External Rotations | Infraspinatus, teres minor (rotator cuff) | Light resistance band | Stand with elbow pinned to side at 90 degrees, band attached at elbow height. | "Rotate forearm outward against band, keeping elbow glued to your ribs. Slow and controlled both directions." | Elbow drifting away from the body, using too heavy a band, moving too fast. | Rotator cuff health is non-negotiable for fighters. The shoulder absorbs enormous stress from punching, posting, and being manipulated in grappling. This keeps the external rotators strong enough to balance the dominant internal rotators. |
| Face Pulls | — | — | — | — | — | See Upper Body Accessories section above. |
| 90/90 Hip Switches | Hip internal and external rotators, glutes | Bodyweight (mat) | Sit on floor with one leg in front (shin perpendicular, externally rotated) and one behind (shin perpendicular, internally rotated). Both knees at 90 degrees. | "Rotate both legs to the opposite position in one smooth motion. Keep chest tall. Pause at each end position." | Leaning back, using hands excessively, rushing through transitions, knees not staying at 90 degrees. | Hip mobility in both internal and external rotation — essential for guard play, sweeps, hip escapes, and kicking. Fighters with restricted hips compensate with their spine, leading to back injuries. |
| Hip CARs (Controlled Articular Rotations) | Hip joint (all surrounding musculature) | Bodyweight | On hands and knees (or standing). Lift one knee to the side. | "Make the largest possible circle with your knee — up, out, back, and around — using only the hip joint. Move slowly and with maximum tension. Reverse direction." | Using momentum, moving the spine to fake hip range, rushing, not engaging max tension. | Maintaining full usable hip range of motion. Fighters lose hip mobility over time from tight guards, repeated impact, and heavy squatting. CARs preserve the range needed for high kicks, rubber guard, and deep half guard. |
| Thoracic Spine Rotations (Open Books) | Thoracic spine rotators, obliques | Bodyweight (mat) | Lie on your side, knees stacked and bent at 90 degrees. Arms extended in front, palms together. | "Rotate the top arm up and over, opening the chest to the ceiling. Follow the hand with your eyes. Let the thoracic spine rotate — keep knees glued together." | Rotating from the lumbar spine (knees separate), not fully opening, rushing. | Thoracic rotation is the engine of punching power and shot defense. Fighters who lose T-spine rotation compensate with the lower back and shoulders, leading to chronic pain. This preserves the rotation needed for hooks, elbows, and looking over the shoulder to defend takedowns. |
| Goblet Squat | Quads, glutes, core | Kettlebell or dumbbell | Hold weight at chest height, elbows inside knees. Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out. | "Sit between your hips, elbows push knees out at the bottom. Chest tall, drive through whole foot." | Leaning forward, heels rising, not hitting depth. | Warm-up and movement prep for squatting patterns. Teaches an upright squat position that transfers to posture in the clinch and double-leg finishes. |
| Glute Bridge | Glutes, hamstrings | Bodyweight or barbell (mat) | Lie on back, knees bent, feet flat on floor hip-width apart. Arms at sides. | "Drive hips to ceiling by squeezing glutes. Hold for a beat at the top. Lower with control." | Hyperextending the lower back, driving through toes instead of heels, not achieving full hip extension. | Hip extension activation for warm-ups. Directly mimics the bridge escape from bottom mount — one of the most fundamental grappling movements. |
| Band Dislocates | Shoulders, rotator cuff, pecs | Resistance band or PVC pipe | Hold band/pipe with wide overhand grip in front of hips. | "Keeping arms straight, raise the band overhead and behind your back in a slow arc. Reverse. Widen grip if needed." | Bending elbows, going too fast, using too narrow a grip before mobility allows. | Full shoulder range-of-motion warm-up. Prepares the shoulder capsule for pressing and overhead work while counteracting the tight anterior shoulders fighters develop from punching and clinch work. |
| Scap Push-Ups | Serratus anterior, lower traps | Bodyweight | Standard push-up position with arms locked. | "Without bending the elbows, protract shoulder blades (push floor away) then retract (let chest sink). Small controlled movement." | Bending the elbows (turning it into a push-up), moving too fast, excessive range. | Serratus anterior activation for scapular stability. Protects the shoulder during pressing and posting — especially important for fighters who spend time on their hands in grappling. |
| PVC Overhead Squat | Shoulders, thoracic spine, hips, ankles | PVC pipe or broomstick | Hold PVC overhead with wide snatch-width grip. Feet shoulder-width. | "Squat to full depth while keeping the PVC directly overhead and arms locked. Chest tall, heels down." | Bar drifting forward, heels lifting, losing thoracic extension, excessive forward lean. | Full-body mobility diagnostic and warm-up. Reveals restrictions in ankles, hips, thoracic spine, and shoulders simultaneously. Builds the overhead stability needed for sprawling with arms extended. |
| KB Halo | Shoulders, core, upper back | Kettlebell | Stand tall, hold KB by the horns upside-down at chest height. | "Circle the KB around your head in a smooth arc, keeping it close. Alternate directions each rep. Brace the core." | Leaning the torso, moving the head instead of the KB, going too fast. | Shoulder warm-up with rotational demand. Prepares shoulders for pressing while building the circular shoulder mobility needed for clinch pummeling and underhook battles. |
| Banded Lateral Walk | Glute medius, hip abductors | Mini band | Place band around ankles or just above knees. Quarter-squat athletic stance. | "Take controlled lateral steps, maintaining tension on the band. Don't let knees cave. Stay low." | Standing too upright, letting the trailing leg snap back, knees collapsing inward. | Glute medius activation for lateral movement stability. Strengthens the hip abductors that stabilize the stance during kicks, lateral cage cutting, and single-leg takedown defense. |
| World's Greatest Stretch | Hip flexors, thoracic spine, hamstrings, groin | Bodyweight | Step into a deep lunge position, place both hands on the floor inside the front foot. | "Rotate the inside arm up toward the ceiling, opening the chest. Hold 2-3 seconds. Return hand to floor. Straighten front leg for hamstring stretch. Repeat other side." | Rushing through positions, not rotating fully, back knee slamming the floor. | The single most efficient dynamic warm-up movement. Addresses hip flexor tightness, thoracic rotation, hamstring length, and groin mobility in one sequence — all areas that take a beating during MMA training. |
| Wall Ankle Stretch | Soleus, Achilles, anterior tibialis | Wall | Place one foot a few inches from a wall, other foot behind for support. Front knee drives forward toward the wall. | "Push the knee over the toes toward the wall without the heel lifting. Move the foot farther back to increase difficulty." | Heel lifting off the ground, knee collapsing inward, not progressing distance. | Ankle dorsiflexion is required for deep squat positions, shooting levels changes, and absorbing force when moving laterally. Restricted ankles cause compensatory knee and hip issues — common in fighters who skip mobility work. |
| Band Pull-Aparts | — | — | — | — | — | See Upper Body Accessories section above. |
| Prone Y/T/W Raises | Lower traps, mid traps, rotator cuff, rear delts | Bodyweight or light dumbbells (2-5 lbs) | Lie face-down on floor or incline bench. Arms hanging. | "Y: raise arms overhead in a Y-shape, thumbs up. T: raise arms to sides in a T. W: pull elbows down and back into a W, squeezing shoulder blades. Hold each top position for 2 seconds." | Using too much weight, shrugging the traps instead of activating lower/mid traps, moving too fast. | Lower and mid trap activation combats the rounded-shoulder posture caused by heavy bag work, grappling, and desk sitting. ## 7. Grip / Neck

| Exercise | Primary Muscles | Equipment | Setup | Execution Cues | Common Mistakes | MMA Benefit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Plate Pinch Holds | Finger flexors, thumb, forearm | Weight plates (two smooth-side-out) | Pinch two plates together (smooth sides out) between fingers and thumb. Stand tall. | "Squeeze the plates as hard as possible. Hold for time. Keep shoulders level." | Using bumper plates (too easy to grip), letting the plates slide, shrugging. | Pinch grip strength for controlling wrists during grappling, maintaining grip on a sweaty opponent, and securing collar ties. The thumb-dominant grip pattern is specific to controlling limbs. |
| Towel Pull-Ups | Lats, forearms, finger flexors | Pull-up bar, two gym towels | Drape towels over the bar, grip one in each hand. | "Perform pull-ups while gripping the towels. Squeeze hard — if your grip fails, the set is over." | Wrapping towels around the hands (defeats the purpose), kipping, cutting range of motion. | Grip endurance on a thick, pliable surface mimics gripping a gi or controlling a sweaty wrist/ankle. The forearm and finger fatigue is directly transferable to late-round grappling grip. |
| Fat Grip Rows | Lats, rhomboids, forearms | Barbell or dumbbells, Fat Gripz / thick bar | Attach Fat Gripz to the bar or use a thick-handle bar. Set up for a standard row. | "Row as normal, but the thick handle forces a much harder grip. Reduce weight by 20-30% compared to normal rows." | Using the same weight as regular rows (grip fails before back), squeezing too tentatively. | Thick-bar training builds the open-hand grip strength used in no-gi grappling, where you cannot close your fingers around a collar or sleeve. Stronger hands mean better wrist control and submission defense. |
| Gi Pull-Ups | Lats, forearms, finger flexors | Pull-up bar, old gi or gi pants | Drape a gi over the pull-up bar. Grip the lapels or fabric. | "Pull up while gripping the gi material. Vary grip positions — lapel, sleeve, collar. Hold at the top for extra credit." | Using thin fabric that tears, wrapping the gi around hands instead of gripping, partial range of motion. | The most sport-specific grip exercise for gi-based grappling. Even in no-gi and MMA, the wrist-and-fabric-type grip endurance transfers to controlling rashguards and maintaining wrist control. |
| Neck Harness (4-Way) | Sternocleidomastoid, upper traps, neck extensors, lateral flexors | Neck harness, weight plate or loading pin | Attach weight to harness. Perform flexion (chin to chest), extension (look up), and lateral flexion (ear to shoulder) from a seated or standing hinged position. | "Controlled range of motion through the full movement. 2-second hold at end range. Start very light (5-10 lbs) and add load slowly over weeks." | Going too heavy too soon, jerking the weight, hyperextending at end range, skipping warm-up sets. | A strong neck directly reduces concussion risk by limiting rapid head acceleration from strikes. Neck strength also resists guillotines, can openers, and cranks. This is the most injury-preventive exercise a fighter can do. |
| Band Neck Isometrics | All neck musculature | Resistance band | Loop band around the head (forehead for extension resistance, back of head for flexion resistance, side for lateral). Anchor band at head height. | "Hold head in a neutral position against the band's pull. 10-20 second holds. Rotate through all four directions." | Band too heavy (neck compensates with torso movement), not training all four directions, holding breath. | Low-impact neck strengthening ideal for daily training. Builds isometric neck endurance — the type of strength needed to resist a head-position change in real time when being punched or caught in a front headlock. Safe enough to perform at every session. |

## 8. Conditioning Modalities

| Exercise | Primary Muscles | Equipment | Setup | Execution Cues | Common Mistakes | MMA Benefit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Assault Bike Intervals | Quads, hamstrings, shoulders, cardiovascular system | Assault / air bike | Adjust seat height so there's a slight bend in the knee at the bottom. Grip the handles. | "Push and pull with arms, drive with legs. For intervals: all-out effort for the work period, easy spin for rest. Focus on RPM or calories as your target." | Sitting too high (hip rocking), arms and legs not coordinated, not going truly all-out during work intervals. | Whole-body energy system training with zero impact. The concurrent arm and leg work mimics the metabolic demand of fighting. Intervals (e.g., 10s on / 20s off) replicate the explosive-burst-then-recover pattern of MMA rounds. |
| Rowing — 500m Repeats | Legs, back, arms, cardiovascular system | Rowing ergometer (Concept2) | Strap feet in, set damper to 4-6. Start with arms extended, shins vertical. | "Drive with legs first, then open the hips, then pull handle to sternum. Reverse the order on the recovery. Target consistent split times across repeats." | Pulling with the arms first (should be legs-hips-arms), setting damper too high, inconsistent pacing. | Full posterior chain conditioning with a pulling emphasis — balances the push-dominant demands of striking. 500m repeats at race pace build the lactic tolerance needed for scramble-heavy rounds and late takedown battles. |
| Jump Rope | Calves, shoulders, coordination, cardiovascular system | Speed rope | Hold handles at hip height, elbows tight to body. Stand on balls of feet. | "Small bounces — just enough to clear the rope. Turn from the wrists, not the shoulders. Stay relaxed." | Jumping too high, turning from the elbows, tension in the shoulders, double-bouncing. | Footwork coordination, calf endurance, and rhythm — the foundation of striking movement. Builds the light, bouncy base that allows fighters to maintain distance, change angles, and stay on the balls of their feet for five rounds. |
| Sled Push / Pull | Quads, glutes, hamstrings, calves, core | Sled, turf or smooth surface | Load the sled. For pushes: hands on high or low handles, lean in. For pulls: attach a strap or rope and face the sled. | "Push: drive with legs, keep arms extended, stay low. Pull: sit back and row hand-over-hand, or backpedal with the strap over your shoulders." | Standing too upright on pushes (losing drive angle), loading too heavy and losing speed, resting between steps. | Zero eccentric load means heavy conditioning without delayed-onset soreness — critical for fighters who need to train skill work the next day. Pushing mimics cage driving; pulling mimics dragging an opponent. Both build leg drive capacity. |
| Sledgehammer Tire Strikes | Core, shoulders, lats, grip, cardiovascular system | Sledgehammer (10-20 lbs), large tire | Stand in a staggered stance facing the tire, hammer overhead with a slight chop angle. | "Slide the top hand up during the lift, slide it down to meet the bottom hand during the strike. Rotate hips into each swing. Alternate sides." | Gripping too tight (grip fatigue), all arms / no hip rotation, not alternating lead side. | Rotational power-endurance — the ability to throw powerful, hip-driven strikes repeatedly. The tool's weight and deceleration demand mirror the impact absorption of hitting pads or an opponent. Builds fight-specific grip endurance. |
| Shadow Boxing | Shoulders, core, legs, cardiovascular system | None (gloves optional) | Fight stance, hands up, chin tucked, weight on balls of feet. | "Throw combinations with intent. Move your feet between combos. Visualize an opponent — don't just wave your hands. Work defense between offense." | Dropping the hands, flat-footed movement, no defensive movement between combos, punching with no purpose. | The most specific conditioning modality for striking. Allows technical practice under fatigue. When used in training programs, shadow boxing rounds serve as active recovery while reinforcing technique and ring generalship. |
| Bag Work | Shoulders, core, legs, arms, cardiovascular system | Heavy bag (70-100 lbs), bag gloves or MMA gloves, hand wraps | Wrap hands, glove up. Set up at punching range from the bag. Fight stance. | "Throw real combinations — jab-cross-hook, not single shots. Move your head and feet between combos. Hit through the bag, not at it." | Standing flat-footed and winging punches, not moving between combos, hitting with bad wrist alignment, going 100% every round without variation. | Full-contact striking conditioning that builds punch endurance, timing, and the ability to maintain technique under fatigue. Programmed in 3-5 minute rounds to match fight timing. The bag's feedback teaches fighters to commit to their shots. |
| Sprawl-to-Burpee | Full body (hip flexors, quads, chest, shoulders, cardiovascular system) | Bodyweight | Stand in fight stance. | "Drop into a sprawl (hips to floor, legs back, chest up), immediately pop back to feet, then jump (or skip the jump for speed). Reset stance and repeat." | Lazy sprawls (hips stay high — defeats the purpose), not returning to a fight stance, pacing too slow for conditioning effect. | The most MMA-specific bodyweight conditioning movement. Combines takedown defense (the sprawl) with explosive recovery to standing. High-rep sets build the anaerobic endurance to stuff takedowns in the championship rounds when most fights are decided. |
