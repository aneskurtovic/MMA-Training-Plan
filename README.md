## Training Philosophy

This program draws from several proven methodologies, combined into a cohesive framework for MMA fighter development.

### Concurrent Training Model (Alex Viada)
Develop multiple physical qualities — strength, power, endurance, and sport-specific conditioning — simultaneously within the same training week. Sessions are sequenced to minimize interference between competing adaptations (e.g., heavy strength is separated from high-volume endurance by at least 6 hours).

### Energy Systems Development (Joel Jamieson)
Build a robust aerobic base through cardiac output work (sustained heart rate at 130-150 BPM for 30-60 minutes). Once the aerobic floor is established, layer in anaerobic capacity work through intervals and fight-specific circuits. A strong aerobic base accelerates recovery between rounds and between sessions.

### Strength Without Mass (Pavel Tsatsouline)
Prioritize neural adaptations over hypertrophy. Use low rep ranges (1-5 reps), submaximal loads (70-85% 1RM), and longer rest periods (3-5 minutes). Avoid training to failure. This builds maximal strength and rate of force development without adding bodyweight that could push past the target weight class.

### Triphasic Training Elements (Cal Dietz)
Structure strength work through three contraction phases:
- **Eccentric** — controlled lowering (3-5 second tempos) to build force absorption capacity and tendon resilience
- **Isometric** — paused reps at sticking points to develop positional strength and stability
- **Concentric** — explosive lifting to develop power output and rate of force development

### Volume Over Intensity for Skill Work (Firas Zahabi)
Train MMA skills frequently but stay below the injury threshold. Most technical sessions should feel like a 6-7 out of 10 effort. Hard sparring is limited to once per week. This approach accumulates more total skill reps over time while keeping the athlete healthy and available for training.

### Functional Movement (Mike Boyle)
Emphasize single-leg exercises (Bulgarian split squats, single-leg RDLs, lunges) to address bilateral deficits and mirror the demands of fighting. Core work focuses on anti-rotation, anti-extension, and anti-lateral flexion rather than crunches — the core's primary job in fighting is to resist unwanted movement.

### Prehab Focus (Eric Cressey)
Every session includes dedicated prehab work: shoulder health (band pull-aparts, face pulls, external rotations), hip mobility (90/90 switches, hip CARs, adductor work), and ankle/wrist mobility. Prevention is built into the warm-up, not treated as an afterthought.

### Fighter-Specific Programming (Phil Daru)
Compound movements are selected for direct cage carryover. Exercises mimic the force vectors, joint angles, and energy demands of fighting. Landmine presses for clinch work, hip escapes loaded with bands, Turkish get-ups for ground-to-standing transitions.

## How to Read the Plans

### RPE Scale (Rate of Perceived Exertion)

| RPE | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| 1-2 | Very light — barely any effort |
| 3-4 | Light — easy conversation, warm-up intensity |
| 5-6 | Moderate — steady effort, can talk in short sentences |
| 7 | Hard — challenging, could do 3 more reps |
| 8 | Very hard — could do 1-2 more reps |
| 9 | Near maximal — maybe 1 more rep with good form |
| 10 | Maximal — absolute limit, form may break |

### Percentage-Based Loading

All barbell lifts are programmed as a percentage of your estimated 1-rep max (1RM). For example, "Back Squat 4x3 @ 80%" means 4 sets of 3 reps at 80% of your Back Squat 1RM (120 kg in this case).

### Tempo Notation

Written as four numbers: **Eccentric-Pause at Bottom-Concentric-Pause at Top**

Example: **3-1-1-0**
- **3** = 3 seconds lowering the weight (eccentric)
- **1** = 1 second pause at the bottom position
- **1** = 1 second lifting the weight (concentric)
- **0** = 0 seconds pause at the top before the next rep

An **X** in the concentric position means explosive (move as fast as possible).

### Heart Rate Zones

| Zone | Name | BPM Range | Feel |
|------|------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1 | Recovery | 100-120 BPM | Very easy, can hold full conversation |
| Zone 2 | Aerobic Base | 130-150 BPM | Comfortable, could sustain for 60+ min |
| Zone 3 | Tempo | 150-165 BPM | Moderately hard, conversation in fragments |
| Zone 4 | Threshold | 165-180 BPM | Hard, can only speak a few words |
| Zone 5 | Max Effort | 180+ BPM | All-out, unsustainable beyond 30-60 seconds |

*BPM ranges are approximate for a trained male athlete aged 25-35. Adjust based on individual max heart rate.*

### Session Intensity Markers

- 🟢 **Easy** — Recovery pace, low CNS demand, should feel refreshed after
- 🟡 **Moderate** — Working hard but controlled, manageable fatigue
- 🔴 **Hard** — High demand, significant fatigue expected, plan recovery accordingly

## Recommended Tracking

| Tool | Purpose | Recommendation |
|------|---------|----------------|
| Heart Rate Monitor | Session intensity, cardiac output pacing, recovery | Polar H10 or Garmin HRM-Pro (chest strap for accuracy) |
| Training Log | Track loads, sets, reps, and session notes | Google Sheets, Strong app, or a physical notebook |
| RPE Tracking | Monitor fatigue and readiness each session | Log RPE for every working set and an overall session RPE |
| Weekly Weigh-ins | Track body composition trends | Same conditions every time: morning, fasted, post-bathroom |
| Resting Heart Rate | Monitor recovery and overtraining risk | Measure upon waking before getting out of bed, log daily |

A rising resting heart rate (5-7+ BPM above baseline over several days) is an early warning sign of accumulated fatigue or illness. Consider backing off training if this occurs.
